Thousands of California families are apostilling critical documents right now — whether for an upcoming visa application, a planned move abroad, or as a protective measure in case of family separation.

An apostille makes your U.S. document legally recognized in any country that is part of the Hague Apostille Convention. Without it, foreign governments, courts, and schools may reject your paperwork entirely — even if the document itself is perfectly valid.

For immigration-related documents, the stakes are higher and the timelines are tighter. A rejected or delayed apostille can hold up a visa, a custody arrangement, or a property transfer at the worst possible time.

Documents We Handle

Common Immigration Documents That Need an Apostille

These are the documents we process most often for immigration, visa, and family protection purposes. If your document isn't listed here, call or text us — we almost certainly handle it.

Birth Certificates

Required for visa applications, citizenship claims, school enrollment abroad, and custody proceedings. Must be a certified copy from the county or state vital records office with original registrar signature and seal.

Marriage Certificates

Needed to prove marital status for spousal visas, property transfers, and name changes abroad. Certified county copy required — church or ceremony certificates are not accepted.

Criminal Background Checks

FBI and California DOJ background checks are commonly required for immigration and work visas. FBI checks require federal apostille through the U.S. Department of State; CA DOJ checks go through the Secretary of State.

Powers of Attorney

Grants someone authority to act on your behalf for legal, financial, or medical decisions. Must be notarized before apostille. Critical for families planning for possible separation.

Custody Agreements

Court-ordered custody documents apostilled for recognition in a foreign country. Ensures legal guardianship rights are honored internationally if a parent is detained or deported.

Divorce Decrees

Certified court copies of final divorce judgments, needed to prove marital status for remarriage or immigration purposes abroad.

Death Certificates

Required for inheritance claims, pension transfers, and insurance processing in foreign countries. Certified copy from the county or state vital records office required.

School & Education Records

Diplomas, transcripts, and teaching credentials apostilled for enrollment or employment abroad. Confirm with the destination whether they need the transcript, the diploma, or both.

Processing Timelines

How Long Does an Immigration Apostille Take?

Timelines depend on the type of document and which government office processes it. Here are current estimates.

California State Documents

  • Same-day rush: Available for California Secretary of State apostilles. See same-day options.
  • Standard mail-in: 3 to 10 business days through the California Secretary of State.
  • Includes: Birth certificates, marriage certificates, death certificates, CA DOJ background checks, and any document notarized by a California notary.

Federal Documents

  • U.S. Department of State: 4 to 8 weeks for federal apostille or authentication.
  • Includes: FBI background checks, documents issued by federal agencies, and documents destined for non-Hague countries requiring federal authentication.

Consular Legalization

  • Timeline varies: Depends on the specific embassy or consulate. Some process in days; others take weeks.
  • Required for: Non-Hague countries (e.g., Taiwan, Thailand, Cuba, UAE). We coordinate the full chain — state or federal authentication followed by consular legalization.
Apostille vs. Legalization

Does Your Country Need an Apostille or Consular Legalization?

The process depends on whether your destination country is a member of the Hague Apostille Convention.

Hague Countries (Apostille)

If your destination country is a Hague member, you need a state apostille (for state-issued documents) or a federal apostille (for federal documents). This is a single-step certification.

Common Hague destinations: Mexico, Colombia, El Salvador, Honduras, Guatemala, Peru, Ecuador, Philippines, India, South Korea, Germany, Spain, Italy, France, UK, Australia, Japan, Brazil, Argentina.

Non-Hague Countries (Legalization)

If your destination country is not a Hague member, you need consular legalization — a multi-step process that includes state or federal authentication followed by legalization at the destination country's embassy or consulate in the U.S.

Common non-Hague destinations: Taiwan, Thailand, Cuba, UAE, Qatar, Canada (some provinces), Vietnam, Egypt.

Immigration Apostille FAQ

Which immigration documents need an apostille?

The most common are birth certificates, marriage certificates, divorce decrees, criminal background checks (FBI or state DOJ), powers of attorney, custody agreements, and adoption papers. The exact list depends on your destination country and the type of visa or legal process.

How long does it take?

California state apostilles take 3 to 10 business days by mail, with same-day rush available. Federal apostilles through the U.S. Department of State take 4 to 8 weeks. Consular legalization timelines vary by embassy.

How much does it cost?

The California Secretary of State charges $20 per document. Our service fee covers document review, preparation, submission, and return shipping. Total cost depends on the number of documents, whether notarization is needed, and the type of processing. Call or text (213) 933‑2507 for an exact quote.

Can you handle documents from other states?

Yes. Each document must be apostilled by the Secretary of State in the state where it was issued or notarized. We coordinate that process regardless of the state.

Do I also need a translation?

Many countries require a certified or sworn translation alongside the apostille. Some countries require a specific type of sworn translator. We can connect you with qualified translators or coordinate translation as part of your order.

What if my document gets rejected?

Rejections usually happen because of incorrect notarization, missing seals, outdated records, or submitting to the wrong office. We review every document before submission specifically to prevent this. If something needs to be corrected, we catch it early and help you fix it.

Can I mail my documents to you?

Yes. Many of our clients are outside the LA area or prefer to mail documents. We walk you through secure shipping options and confirm everything by phone, text, or email before you send anything.

I'm preparing for possible family separation. Can you help?

Yes. Many families in mixed-status households are proactively apostilling custody agreements, powers of attorney, and vital records. We handle these cases with urgency and discretion. You don't need to explain your circumstances — just tell us what you need and where it's going.
