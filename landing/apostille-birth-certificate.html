Why Birth Certificates

The Most Common Document We Apostille

Birth certificates are the single most frequently apostilled document we handle. They are required for almost every international process that involves proving identity, citizenship, or family relationships.

Common reasons clients need this

  • Dual citizenship applications — proving U.S. birth to claim citizenship in a parent's or grandparent's country of origin.
  • Immigration petitions — sponsoring family members or establishing identity for visa and residency applications.
  • Marriage abroad — many countries require an apostilled birth certificate before issuing a marriage license to a foreigner.
  • School enrollment overseas — registering children in foreign schools often requires an apostilled birth record.
  • Foreign property and inheritance — proving family relationships for estate claims, property transfers, and pension benefits abroad.

What gets people stuck

  • Using a hospital souvenir certificate instead of an official certified copy — the Secretary of State will reject it immediately.
  • Submitting a photocopy or home-printed scan instead of an original certified copy with a raised or stamped seal.
  • Not realizing the birth certificate must be apostilled by the state that issued it, not the state where you currently live.
  • Ordering a certified copy but getting one with a faint or missing registrar signature — this can trigger rejection.
  • Waiting until the last minute and discovering the Secretary of State is backed up for weeks during peak season.

Before You Start

What Will and Won't Be Accepted

The Secretary of State will only apostille a birth certificate that meets specific format requirements. Here's what works and what doesn't.

✅ Will be accepted

  • Certified copy from the state vital records office or county recorder with registrar seal and signature.
  • Recently issued certified copies — especially helpful if the foreign office requires a record less than 6 or 12 months old.
  • Certified copies with a clear, legible seal — raised, stamped, or embossed.
  • Multiple certified copies if you need apostilles for more than one country or purpose (each apostille attaches to one document).

❌ Will be rejected

  • Hospital souvenir certificates — decorative certificates with baby footprints, given at the hospital. These are not official records.
  • Photocopies or scanned printouts — even if the original was legitimate, a photocopy is not a certified copy.
  • Certificates with missing or illegible seals — faded ink stamps or absent embossed seals.
  • Certificates issued by a different state submitted to the wrong Secretary of State — each state only apostilles its own documents.
  • "Informational" copies marked "not valid for legal purposes" — some states issue these by default if you don't specifically request a certified copy.
How Long It Takes

Birth Certificate Apostille Timelines

Actual processing times depend on which state issued your birth certificate and whether you need additional steps like consular legalization.

California birth certificates

  • Same-day or next business day — available with in-person filing at the California Secretary of State when your case qualifies for rush processing.
  • 1–3 business days — standard in-person filing turnaround during normal volume periods.
  • 1–2 weeks by mail — if in-person filing is not used, mail processing times vary based on volume and holidays.

Other states

  • 3–10 business days — most states process apostilles within this window by mail, with some offering expedited options.
  • Walk-in / in-person — some states allow same-day or next-day service if we appear in person or use an authorized agent.
  • Add time for consular legalization — non-Hague countries require U.S. Department of State authentication and embassy legalization, which can add several weeks.
