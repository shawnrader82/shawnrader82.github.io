Philippines apostille FAQ

Questions about apostilling documents for the Philippines.

Does the Philippines accept apostilles instead of consulate authentication?

Yes. The Philippines acceded to the Hague Apostille Convention in May 2019. As of then, properly notarized U.S. documents bearing a California Secretary of State apostille are accepted by Philippine government agencies, banks, and registries without separate consulate (red ribbon) authentication. Some private entities still ask for consularization out of habit \u2014 we can confirm what your specific recipient in the Philippines requires.

What is the difference between an apostille and the old red ribbon?

Before 2019, U.S. documents needed authentication by the Philippine Consulate \u2014 a stamp and red ribbon often called \u201credribboned.\u201d After the Philippines joined the Hague Convention, the apostille (a single page issued by the California Secretary of State) replaced the red ribbon for most uses. The apostille is faster, cheaper, and accepted by PSA, LRA, DFA, BIR, courts, and Philippine banks.

Can you use my SPA template from my lawyer in the Philippines?

Yes. You can bring the Special Power of Attorney template provided by your attorney, family, or barangay in the Philippines. We review the notary section to make sure it meets California acknowledgment requirements and suggest adjustments before your appointment so the document is accepted at both the California apostille office and the receiving Philippine agency.

Which Philippine agencies most commonly require an apostille?

Most apostilled documents we handle are filed with the Philippine Statistics Authority (PSA) for civil records, the Land Registration Authority (LRA) and Registry of Deeds for property transfers, the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) for estate tax and donor\u2019s tax, and Philippine banks for account and inheritance matters. The Department of Foreign Affairs (DFA) Office of Consular Affairs is the issuing apostille authority on the Philippine side, not a receiving one.

Can multiple signers (spouse, siblings, heirs) sign in one visit?

Yes. We frequently handle Filipino families where a spouse, siblings, or heirs sign an SPA, extra-judicial settlement, or deed of donation together. Each signer presents valid ID, signs in front of the notary, and the document is then routed to the California Secretary of State for apostille. If signers cannot meet at once, we can do separate visits as long as the document allows.
