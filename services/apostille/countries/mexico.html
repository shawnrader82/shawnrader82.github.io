Common reasons people need Mexico apostilles

Why Los Angeles clients apostille documents for Mexico.

Mexico accepts U.S.-origin documents with a California Secretary of State apostille — no additional authentication needed. Spanish translation by a Mexican perito traductor is required before Mexican authorities process them. Here are the most common situations we handle.

1

Dual citizenship & Mexican residency

U.S.-born children of Mexican parents register dual citizenship through the Registro Civil or Mexican Consulate using an apostilled U.S. birth certificate. INM residency applications (Residente Temporal / Permanente) also require apostilled birth and marriage certificates.

2

Mexican property & notario público

U.S. clients purchasing, selling, or gifting real property in Mexico must present apostilled identity and title documents to the Mexican notario público executing the escritura pública. Powers of attorney authorize a Mexican representative to act on your behalf without traveling.

3

Registro Civil marriages & civil status

Foreign nationals marrying in Mexico must present apostilled civil status documentation to the Registro Civil — a single-status affidavit for never-married individuals, and an apostilled divorce decree or death certificate for those previously married.

4

Estate succession & inheritance

U.S.-based heirs of Mexican estates present apostilled death certificates, birth certificates, and marriage certificates to the Mexican notario público or family court handling succession — to claim inheritance rights and transfer property titles.

Mexico apostille FAQ

Questions about apostilling documents for Mexico.

Does Mexico accept a California apostille?

Yes. Mexico joined the Hague Apostille Convention in 1995. A California Secretary of State apostille is the correct and sufficient U.S.-side authentication for California-issued documents used in Mexico. No Mexican consulate or U.S. Embassy authentication is required. Spanish translation by a Mexican perito traductor is typically required before the Mexican authority will process the document.

Do apostilled documents need to be translated into Spanish for Mexico?

Yes, typically. Mexico requires that foreign-language documents be accompanied by a certified Spanish translation by a Mexican perito traductor (court-certified translator) before use at Registro Civil, INM (immigration), or Mexican notario público offices. We handle the California apostille in Los Angeles; you arrange the Spanish translation in Mexico after receiving the apostilled original.

Can I sign a power of attorney at the Mexican Consulate in Los Angeles instead?

Yes — the Mexican Consulate General in Los Angeles offers consular notarization (notarización consular) for some documents, including powers of attorney. Mexican authorities may accept consular notarization without the California apostille chain. However, consulate appointments can be limited. Confirm with your Mexican notario público or attorney which path they prefer before booking either option.

What is a perito traductor and why does Mexico require one?

A perito traductor is a court-certified translator authorized by a Mexican state or federal court to produce certified translations of foreign documents. Mexican law requires that foreign-language documents presented to government offices be accompanied by a certified translation by a perito traductor — not just any bilingual person or general translation service. We note where a perito traductor is needed but do not provide translation services.

How long does a Mexico apostille take from Los Angeles?

California Secretary of State apostille typically takes 2–5 business days. Rush service is available when INM appointments or Mexican property closing dates are imminent. Shipping to Mexico adds 3–5 business days via tracked courier. Spanish translation by a perito traductor is arranged separately in Mexico and adds additional time.
