What this page covers

Special Power of Attorney Apostille, end to end.

A Special Power of Attorney lets a family member, attorney, or trusted representative in the Philippines legally act on your behalf for a specific transaction — most often selling property, closing a bank account, claiming a pension, settling an estate, or representing you before PSA, LRA, BIR, or a Philippine court. We confirm the SPA wording matches what the receiving agency or bank expects, notarize it in a single mobile visit anywhere in Los Angeles County, and apostille it through the California Secretary of State so it is accepted in the Philippines without the old consulate red ribbon.

Common reasons people need this

When a Special Power of Attorney apostille is required.

Property sale or transfer

Authorize an attorney-in-fact to sell, buy, lease, or transfer real property in the Philippines, sign the deed of absolute sale, accept payment, and process title transfer through the Registry of Deeds and LRA.

Banking & estate matters

Open, close, or manage Philippine bank accounts, claim deposits of deceased relatives, withdraw inheritance funds, or sign on behalf of an OFW family member for estate-related banking transactions.

PSA, BIR & LRA filings

Authorize someone to claim PSA civil records, file or receive BIR documents (TIN, estate or donor’s tax), or transact with the Land Registration Authority and Registry of Deeds on your behalf.

Family & legal affairs

Empower a relative or counsel to file legal documents, represent you in court, settle inheritance disputes, sign affidavits, or process school, immigration, or pension matters for you in the Philippines.

What will and won't be accepted

Accepted & rejected Special Power of Attorney copies.

Will be accepted

  • Drafted SPA with specific powers granted (sell, mortgage, claim, withdraw, file, etc.)
  • Signer present with current government-issued photo ID
  • California acknowledgment block (most SPAs use acknowledgment, not jurat)
  • Full legal name of signer matching ID exactly
  • Clear identification of the attorney-in-fact (full name, address, relationship)
  • Specific property, account, or matter referenced (lot number, account number, deceased’s name)

Will be rejected

  • Pre-signed SPAs — signer must sign in front of the California notary
  • SPAs with only a jurat where an acknowledgment is required (or vice versa)
  • Vague language like “all matters” without listing specific powers (often rejected at PSA/LRA)
  • Photocopies of the signed SPA — the apostille goes on the original notarized document
  • Expired or non-photo IDs (driver’s license, U.S. passport, or PH passport must be current)

  • California notaries cannot draft the SPA — we notarize what you bring. Get the template from your lawyer, family, or barangay in the Philippines.
  • Most SPAs use an acknowledgment. If the SPA includes a sworn statement (e.g., “I declare under penalty of perjury…”), we use a jurat instead.

  • Once apostilled, the SPA does not need to go through the Philippine Consulate. The apostille replaced the red ribbon for Philippine use in 2019.

Special Power of Attorney Apostille FAQ

Frequently asked questions.

Do I still need the red ribbon from the Philippine Consulate?

No. The Philippines joined the Hague Apostille Convention in May 2019. As of then, the California Secretary of State apostille replaces the old Philippine Consulate red-ribbon authentication for almost all uses. Some private institutions may still ask out of habit — confirm with the specific PSA, LRA, BIR, bank, or law office that will use the SPA.

What’s the difference between a General and Special Power of Attorney for the Philippines?

A General Power of Attorney grants broad authority over your affairs; a Special Power of Attorney grants authority for one specific transaction or limited set of transactions — such as selling a named property or withdrawing a specific bank account. Philippine agencies and banks almost always require an SPA (specific) rather than a general one for real estate, banking, and estate matters.

Can my SPA list multiple specific powers in one document?

Yes, and this is common. A single SPA can authorize your attorney-in-fact to sell a property, transact with the related bank, and file the BIR documents — as long as each power is specifically described. Listing powers clearly (with property addresses, lot numbers, account numbers, and PSA references) reduces the chance of rejection.

Does the apostille translate the SPA into Filipino?

No. The apostille only certifies the California notary’s signature and seal — it does not translate or change the document. SPAs for the Philippines are accepted in English, so translation is rarely needed.

Can my spouse and I sign the SPA together at the same visit?

Yes. We frequently notarize SPAs for spouses, siblings, or co-owners in one mobile visit. Each signer presents their own valid photo ID and signs before the notary. If a co-owner is in the Philippines or out of state, they must notarize separately in their own jurisdiction.

How long is an apostilled SPA valid in the Philippines?

The apostille itself does not expire. However, many Philippine banks, registries, and law offices prefer SPAs notarized within the past six to twelve months — some require notarization within the past 30 days for high-value transactions. Confirm the receiving institution’s policy before you book.
