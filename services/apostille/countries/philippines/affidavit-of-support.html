An Affidavit of Support or Consent is a sworn statement — you swear under penalty of perjury that the facts are true. California requires a jurat (not an acknowledgment) for affidavits. The California Secretary of State applies the apostille so the document is accepted by Philippine embassies, PSA civil registry, DFA, U.S. immigration consular offices, and Philippine schools or agencies.

Affidavits of support for a relative’s visa application — U.S. tourist or immigrant visas, Philippine 13(a) spouse visas, student visas, or special resident retiree visas — documenting your income, assets, and commitment to support the applicant.

Marriage & CENOMAR support

Affidavits filed alongside Philippine marriage license applications, often with CENOMAR (Certificate of No Marriage), affidavits of single status, parental consent for marriage, or sponsor support for fiancé / fiancée visas.

Financial & school support

Affidavits of financial support for a Philippine-based student, dependent relative, or family member — used by schools, immigration, and the receiving agency to verify ongoing commitment.

What will and won't be accepted

Accepted & rejected Affidavit of Support / Consent copies.

Will be accepted

  • Drafted affidavit with sworn-statement language (under penalty of perjury)
  • Signer present with current government-issued photo ID
  • Jurat block (not an acknowledgment) attached or pre-formatted
  • Specific facts — names, dates, amounts, relationships — clearly stated
  • Full legal name of signer matching ID
  • Supporting attachments (tax returns, pay stubs, bank statements) listed if referenced

Will be rejected

  • Pre-signed affidavits — signer must sign and swear in front of the notary
  • Affidavits with acknowledgment instead of jurat
  • Vague or opinion-based statements (must be facts within personal knowledge)
  • Expired or non-photo IDs
  • Drafts in Filipino without an English version (California notarization is in English)

Good to know

  • A jurat means you swear the contents are true — lying on a notarized affidavit is perjury, a criminal offense in California.
  • California notaries cannot draft the affidavit. Use a template from your immigration attorney, the receiving Philippine agency, the embassy, or your family’s lawyer.
  • For minor travel, the Philippine Bureau of Immigration may also require a DSWD travel clearance — the notarized affidavit of consent supports that process but does not replace it.
  • Since the Philippines joined the Hague Apostille Convention in 2019, the apostille is accepted in place of the old Philippine Consulate red ribbon for almost all uses.

Affidavit of Support / Consent Apostille FAQ

Frequently asked questions.

Is an Affidavit of Support the same as the U.S. immigration I-864?

No. The U.S. immigration Form I-864 is a federal document with its own rules — it does not require apostille because it goes to USCIS. The affidavit of support we notarize and apostille is a separate document used by Philippine agencies (PSA, embassies, schools) or by U.S. consular offices when supporting a Philippine-based applicant. They serve different purposes.

Do I need this for a Philippine marriage license?

Often, yes — especially if one party is a foreign national or has been married before. Philippine civil registries commonly require notarized and apostilled affidavits of support, single status, or parental consent (for parties under 25), in addition to CENOMAR. Confirm with the PSA office or local civil registrar where the marriage license is being issued.

Do I need a DSWD travel clearance for minors going to the Philippines?

For minors traveling alone or with someone other than a parent or legal guardian, the Philippine DSWD often requires a travel clearance. A notarized and apostilled affidavit of parental consent supports the DSWD application but does not replace it. Your PH family or travel agent can confirm whether DSWD clearance is needed.

Can I include financial details (income, savings, employer) in the affidavit?

Yes — affidavits of support often include specific financial information: annual income, employer, savings balance, real estate holdings, and a statement of commitment. Attach supporting documents (tax returns, pay stubs, bank letters) if the receiving agency requires them — they can be referenced in the affidavit and apostilled together as one package.

Can my spouse and I both sign one joint affidavit of support?

Yes — joint affidavits of support (e.g., from both parents supporting a child’s visa) are common. Both signers appear in front of the notary together, present their own IDs, and swear to the truth of the statement. The notary applies a single jurat covering both signatures.

Is an apostilled affidavit enough, or do I still need consularization?

Since the Philippines joined the Hague Apostille Convention in 2019, the apostille is accepted in place of consularization (red ribbon) by Philippine agencies, embassies, and most receiving institutions. Some U.S. consular offices and visa-processing centers still request additional consular notarization in specific cases — confirm with the receiving office before you book.
