The current consular legalization chain

How Thailand document legalization works right now.

Because Thailand has not yet formally entered the Hague Convention, every U.S. document must pass through all three authentication steps before Thai government offices, district offices, and employers will accept it. We manage Steps 1 and 2.

Step 1 — California Secretary of State

  • State-issued documents (birth, marriage, death certificates, court orders) go directly to the California Secretary of State for authentication of the issuing official's seal.
  • Notarized documents (powers of attorney, income letters, affidavits, single-status declarations) must be signed and sealed by a California notary public first, then authenticated by the California Secretary of State.
  • Diplomas and academic records from California institutions typically require notarization of a certified copy before California SOS authentication.
  • Typical processing: 3–10 business days depending on submission method.

Step 2 — U.S. Department of State

  • The U.S. Dept of State in Washington D.C. authenticates the California Secretary of State's seal. The Royal Thai Embassy and Thai Consulates generally require this federal authentication step.
  • Standard: 2–4 weeks. Rush: 3–5 business days.
  • After Step 2, your document is ready for the Royal Thai consular legalization step.

Step 3 — Royal Thai Embassy or Consulate (consular legalization)

  • The Royal Thai Embassy in Washington D.C. or the Royal Thai Consulate-General in Los Angeles applies the final Thai legalization stamp — making the document accepted by Thai government authorities.
  • Each Thai consular post covers specific U.S. states. For California documents, the Royal Thai Consulate-General in Los Angeles typically has jurisdiction — but some document types or Thai government requests may specify the Embassy in D.C. Call us to confirm before submitting.
  • Thai legalization adds approximately 1–2 weeks after the consular office receives your documents, depending on the office and current volume.
  • Many Thai offices require certified Thai translations alongside the legalized original — confirm translation requirements with your Thai attorney or the receiving Thai authority before submitting.

Thailand legalization FAQ

Questions about document legalization for Thailand.

Why doesn't Thailand accept apostilles yet?

Thailand has not yet joined the Hague Apostille Convention, so Thai authorities currently do not accept standard apostilles. The Thai Cabinet approved accession to the Convention on December 9, 2025 — a significant milestone — but the Convention will not enter into force for Thailand until approximately 6–8 months after Thailand formally deposits its instrument of accession with the HCCH. Until that deposit and the waiting period are complete, the traditional consular legalization chain remains required: California Secretary of State → U.S. Department of State → Royal Thai Embassy or Thai Consulate.

What is the current legalization chain to Thailand?

As of May 2026, U.S. documents for Thailand must go through a full three-step consular legalization chain: (1) California Secretary of State authentication of the document or notary seal, (2) U.S. Department of State authentication of the California SOS seal — which the Royal Thai Embassy and consulates generally require — and (3) Royal Thai Embassy in Washington D.C. or Royal Thai Consulate-General in Los Angeles legalization stamp. We handle Steps 1 and 2.

Royal Thai Consulate-General Los Angeles vs. Royal Thai Embassy Washington D.C. — which do I use?

Each Royal Thai consular post in the U.S. serves a specific jurisdiction. For California clients, the Royal Thai Consulate-General in Los Angeles typically handles California-issued documents. However, some document types or specific Thai government requests may direct you to the Royal Thai Embassy in Washington D.C. Sending documents to the wrong office can cause significant delays. Call us before your order so we can confirm the correct consular office for your specific document and its intended use in Thailand.

Do I need a certified Thai translation?

Many Thai government offices — district offices (Amphur), immigration, and professional licensing bodies — require certified Thai translations of English-language U.S. documents, especially for marriage registration, visa applications, and court or property matters. Even where the Royal Thai Embassy accepts English documents at the legalization stage, the Thai authority receiving the document inside Thailand often requires a Thai translation. Confirm translation requirements with the specific Thai office or your Thai attorney before submitting.

How long does Royal Thai legalization take?

The U.S.-side steps we handle take approximately 2–5 weeks total: California Secretary of State authentication is typically 3–10 business days; U.S. Department of State authentication is typically 2–4 weeks standard or 3–5 business days rush. Royal Thai Embassy or Consulate-General legalization then adds approximately 1–2 weeks after they receive your documents, depending on the office and current volume. Budget 5–8 weeks total for the complete chain without rush services.

When will Thailand officially accept apostilles?

Thailand's Cabinet approved accession to the Hague Apostille Convention on December 9, 2025. Under the Convention's rules, Thailand must formally deposit its instrument of accession with the Hague Conference on Private International Law (HCCH), after which the Convention enters into force for Thailand approximately 6–8 months later. As of May 2026, Thailand has not yet deposited that instrument, so no official apostille entry-into-force date has been announced. We will update this page as soon as the apostille route becomes available for Thailand.

Will documents legalized now still be valid after Thailand joins the Hague Convention?

Documents fully legalized through the current consular chain — California Secretary of State → U.S. Department of State → Royal Thai Embassy or Consulate — should remain valid for the purpose they were legalized for, regardless of Thailand's future Hague membership. Hague accession changes the process for future documents; it does not retroactively affect previously legalized ones. If your document is being prepared for a Thai government process that is many months away, call us to discuss timing and whether it makes sense to wait for the apostille route in your particular situation.
