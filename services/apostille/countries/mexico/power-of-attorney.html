What will and won't be accepted

Accepted & rejected power of attorney copies.

Will be accepted

  • POA drafted to Mexican legal standards (by a Mexican attorney or notario is preferred)
  • Specific powers clearly listed (sell property, operate account, represent in court, etc.)
  • Grantor present with current government-issued photo ID
  • California acknowledgment block (most POAs use acknowledgment)

Will be rejected

  • Pre-signed POAs — grantor must sign in front of the California notary
  • Vague “all matters” POAs without specific powers (often rejected by Mexican notarios and courts)
  • Photocopies — apostille attaches to the original notarized document

Good to know

  • Mexican notarios públicos are government-authorized officers very different from U.S. notaries public. They handle property transactions, estate succession, and other major legal instruments. Their requirements for a foreign POA are specific — have your Mexican notario review the draft before you sign.
  • Mexican law often requires POAs to be written in Spanish or in a bilingual format. We notarize the California acknowledgment block on whatever language version your Mexican attorney prepares.
  • The California apostille replaces any need for Mexican consulate or U.S. Embassy legalization. Mexico has been a Hague Convention member since 1995.
  • Some Mexican notarios may require the POA to also be translated into Spanish by a certified perito traductor even if the original is bilingual. Confirm with your Mexican attorney.

Power of Attorney Apostille FAQ — Mexico Use

Frequently asked questions.

Does Mexico accept a California apostille on a power of attorney?

Yes. Mexico joined the Hague Apostille Convention in 1995. A California-notarized and apostilled power of attorney is recognized by Mexican notarios públicos, courts, banks, and government offices without any additional Mexican consulate or U.S. Embassy legalization.

Should my POA be drafted in Spanish for use in Mexico?

Mexican notarios strongly prefer or require a Spanish-language or bilingual POA. Have your Mexican attorney or the notario prepare the draft in Spanish (with or without an English version). We add the California notarial acknowledgment block in English and notarize the document. The apostille is then issued on the notarized instrument.

Can I sign a POA at the Mexican Consulate in Los Angeles instead?

Yes — the Mexican Consulate in Los Angeles offers consular notarization services (a “notarización consular”) for some documents, which may be accepted by Mexican notarios without the California apostille chain. However, consulate appointment availability can be limited and the process differs from a California notarization + apostille. Contact the consulate for their current services and fees, and confirm with your Mexican notario which path they prefer.

My Mexican property closing is in three weeks. Can you rush this?

Rush California apostille service is available and typically takes 1–2 business days. Contact us as soon as possible with the draft POA so we can notarize immediately and submit for rush apostille. Once apostilled, we ship via overnight courier to Mexico.

Does the apostilled POA need a certified Spanish translation?

If the POA is already in Spanish or bilingual, a separate translation may not be needed. If the POA is only in English, some Mexican notarios may require a certified Spanish translation by a perito traductor. Confirm with your Mexican notario before the POA is drafted.

How long is a Mexican POA valid?

In Mexico, powers of attorney do not automatically expire, but Mexican notarios and banks may question documents older than one to two years for high-value transactions. If your Mexican transaction is time-sensitive, prepare the POA close to the date it will be used.
