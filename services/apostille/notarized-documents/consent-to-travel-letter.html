A consent-to-travel letter is a notarized statement from the non-traveling parent (or both parents, when the child travels alone or with another adult). California notarizes the parent’s signature via acknowledgment or jurat, then the California Secretary of State applies the apostille for overseas border officers to accept.

What this page covers

Consent to Travel Letter Apostille, end to end.

When a minor travels internationally without both parents, border officers, airlines, and destination immigration may ask for a notarized consent letter from the absent parent or parents. Apostille is increasingly required — especially for Mexico, Costa Rica, and many European destinations. We confirm the wording matches what the destination expects, notarize it in a single mobile visit, and route it through the California Secretary of State for apostille.

When a consent to travel letter apostille is required.

Minors traveling abroad

Confirm to border and airline staff that a child traveling without both legal parents has documented consent — reducing the risk of delays, denial of boarding, or accusations of child abduction.

Single-parent travel

Document the non-traveling parent’s consent when one parent takes the child on vacation, to visit family, or to a destination requiring formal proof.

Custody or divorce situations

Demonstrate compliance with custody orders requiring documented consent from the other parent before international travel with shared children.

What will and won't be accepted

Accepted & rejected consent to travel letter copies.

Will be accepted

  • Letter listing child’s full name, date of birth, and passport number
  • Travel dates, destination, and accompanying adult’s full name
  • Consenting parent(s) present with current government-issued photo ID
  • Notary acknowledgment or jurat block (depending on destination)
  • Optional medical-decision and emergency-contact authorizations

Will be rejected

  • Letters signed before the notary’s presence
  • Letters missing the child’s identifying details
  • Photocopies of the parent’s signature or ID
  • Expired or non-government IDs
  • Letters drafted vaguely without travel dates or destination

Good to know

  • Some destinations require both parents to sign. Mexico, for example, generally requires consent from both legal parents when a child travels without one.
  • If one parent is deceased, attach a notarized copy of the death certificate. If one parent has sole custody, attach a notarized copy of the custody order.
  • Carry both the original apostilled letter and a copy during travel — some border officers retain the original.

Consent to Travel Letter Apostille FAQ

Frequently asked questions.

Do U.S. border officers require a consent letter when a minor leaves the country?

U.S. exit officers rarely require it, but airlines and destination-country border officers often do. Without it, your child may be denied boarding or entry. The risk is at the destination, not at U.S. departure.

What countries most commonly require apostilled consent letters?

Mexico, Costa Rica, Brazil, South Africa, and several European countries are strict. Many other countries accept un-apostilled notarized letters but increasingly request apostille for non-Hague-country travelers.

Do both parents need to sign?

It depends on the destination. Many countries require consent from both legal parents when a child travels without one of them. Single-parent custody situations may need additional supporting documents (custody order, death certificate).

Can I get a generic consent letter that works for any future trip?

Not really. Most destinations want specific travel dates, destinations, and accompanying-adult details. A generic letter may be rejected. Plan one letter per trip.

Can the apostille be done after the travel date?

No — the apostille must be valid and attached before travel. Plan at least 2–3 weeks ahead, longer for international shipping or non-Hague destinations.

Does the letter need translation for the destination country?

Some destinations require translation. Get the apostille on the English original first, then have a certified translator handle the translation — not before notarization.
