When you need this

Common reasons people apostille court documents.

Most court-document apostille requests involve proving a legal status, enforcing a judgment, or presenting a certified order in another country.

1

Family status abroad

Divorce decrees, custody orders, and name change records are commonly requested for remarriage, family visas, and civil registration abroad.

2

Probate & inheritance

Probate orders and estate judgments may be required when heirs, assets, or real property are located outside the United States.

3

Adoption & guardianship

Foreign authorities may request apostilled adoption decrees or guardianship orders to confirm legal authority and family relationships.

4

Cross-border disputes

Civil judgments and court-certified records may be needed overseas for banking, litigation support, settlement enforcement, or commercial matters.

Before you send anything

Quick checklist for court documents.

A few details upfront help confirm whether your order is ready for apostille or if a fresh certified copy is needed first.

  • Confirm the type of court record you have, such as a divorce decree, probate order, custody order, adoption decree, or civil judgment.
  • Check that the document is a certified copy from the court clerk, not just a printout or file-stamped photocopy.
  • Verify whether all pages are present, including the clerk certification page and seal.
  • Know the state where the court record was issued.
  • Note the destination country and your deadline.
  • Gather any foreign instructions requesting translations, notarized statements, or legalization beyond apostille.

Good to know

  • Most court records do not start with notarization. The apostille typically relies on the court clerk certification already attached to the certified order.
  • Each state apostilles its own court documents, so out-of-state court records cannot usually be apostilled through California.
  • Older court orders, incomplete records, or separated certification pages are common reasons for delay.
  • If the destination country is not in the Hague Apostille Convention, additional legalization steps may be required after state authentication.

Court documents FAQ

Questions about court document apostilles.

Does my court order need to be a certified copy?

Usually yes. Most court records must be certified by the issuing court clerk before an apostille can be issued, and plain copies are typically not accepted.

Can you help if my court record was issued in another state?

Yes. We help determine which state issued the court record, whether a fresh certified copy is needed, and how to route the apostille request through the correct state authority.

Do court documents need notarization first?

Many certified court records go directly to apostille once the clerk certification is in place. Some countries also require translations, supporting affidavits, or further legalization.

What if the court document is old, incomplete, or missing pages?

That is common. We often recommend ordering a fresh certified copy from the issuing court when the record is incomplete, separated, damaged, or likely to be questioned overseas.
