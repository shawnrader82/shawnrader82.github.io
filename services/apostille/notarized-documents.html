Notarized document FAQ

Questions about apostilling notarized documents.

Which notarized documents can you help apostille?

Common notarized documents include powers of attorney, affidavits of single status, financial support affidavits, parental consent-to-travel letters for minors, copy certifications by document custodian, declarations, identity verifications, and other personal documents notarized in California for foreign use.

Do I need a specific notarization type for apostille?

Yes. California notaries perform two main acts: acknowledgments (verifying the signer’s identity and willingness to sign) and jurats (verifying the signer swore the document is true under penalty of perjury). Most powers of attorney use acknowledgments; most affidavits and sworn statements require jurats. We confirm the correct act before notarization so the apostille office accepts it.

Can you draft the notarized document, or just notarize it?

California notaries cannot draft legal documents — that would be unauthorized practice of law. We can notarize a document you bring or have drafted by an attorney, an agency, or downloaded from a foreign consulate. If you need help finding a template, we can point you to consulate-approved formats for common countries.

Does the apostille certify the content of my notarized document?

No. The apostille certifies only the authenticity of the notary’s signature and seal, not the truth or legal effect of the document itself. The receiving country or institution decides whether the content meets their requirements, which is why consulate-approved wording matters.

Can you notarize and apostille documents in the same visit?

We can notarize in a single mobile visit anywhere in Los Angeles County, but the apostille itself is processed by the California Secretary of State and typically takes a few business days. We handle the routing, prepare the correct cover and return materials, and ship the document back to you once apostilled.
