What this page covers

Consular Report of Death Abroad apostille, end to end.

When a U.S. citizen passes away outside the United States, the U.S. embassy or consulate in that country issues a Consular Report of the Death of a U.S. Citizen Abroad (CRODA). This federal record is often required to settle estates, claim life insurance and benefits, transfer remains, and update foreign vital-records offices. Because the CRODA is a federal document issued by the U.S. Department of State, it is authenticated at the federal level — not through a state Secretary of State.

Common reasons people need this

When a Consular Report of Death Abroad apostille is required.

Estate settlement & probate

Probate courts and estate attorneys abroad routinely require an apostilled CRODA before they will distribute property, close bank accounts, or settle a deceased U.S. citizen's foreign holdings.

Insurance & benefits claims

Foreign insurers, pension administrators, and benefits agencies frequently request an apostilled CRODA to confirm date and place of death before paying out claims to surviving beneficiaries.

Remains transfer & repatriation

Funeral homes, mortuary services, and authorities involved in international remains transfer often need an apostilled CRODA as part of the documentation chain for repatriation.

Foreign civil registry updates

Many countries require an apostilled CRODA before updating local marriage, residency, or birth registry records that reference the deceased U.S. citizen.

What will and won't be accepted

Accepted & rejected CRODA copies.

Will be accepted

  • Original Consular Report of Death Abroad issued by a U.S. embassy or consulate
  • Certified replacement copies issued by the U.S. Department of State Vital Records office
  • Amended or corrected CRODAs reissued by the U.S. Department of State
  • Related federal letters or certifications issued alongside the CRODA

Will be rejected

  • Photocopies, scans, or notarized copies of a CRODA (apostille goes on the federal certified copy itself)
  • Foreign hospital death records or local death certificates issued abroad
  • Damaged, laminated, or altered CRODAs with broken seals
  • State-issued death certificates (those go through the state Secretary of State, not federal)

Realistic timelines

Consular Report of Death Abroad apostille processing time.

Hague Apostille countries

  • Federal apostille — issued by the U.S. Department of State Office of Authentications.
  • Standard processing — typically several weeks; current State Department times fluctuate.
  • Replacement CRODA — add 4–8 weeks if a new certified copy must be ordered from the Department of State first.

Non-Hague countries

  • Federal authentication at the U.S. Department of State (same step as apostille).
  • Consular legalization — add 2–6 weeks at the destination country's embassy or consulate in the U.S.
  • We coordinate the full chain so families don't have to chase multiple offices during a difficult time.

  • Type of process abroad (estate, insurance, repatriation, registry)

Good to know

  • Federal, not state: CRODAs are authenticated by the U.S. Department of State, not California or another state Secretary of State.
  • Order multiple copies: international estate cases often need several apostilled originals; order extras up front to avoid rework.
  • Translation: the apostille does not translate the document. Certified translation, if required, happens after apostille.

CRODA apostille FAQ

Frequently asked questions.

Is a Consular Report of Death Abroad the same as a foreign death certificate?

No. A CRODA is a U.S. federal document issued by the Department of State documenting the death of a U.S. citizen abroad. The local death certificate from the country where the death occurred is a separate document and is issued by that country's authorities.

Can I apostille a photocopy of the CRODA?

Foreign authorities generally require an original or certified copy issued by the U.S. Department of State. We help you determine whether your current CRODA is suitable or whether you should request a new certified copy specifically for apostille.

What if I never received an original CRODA?

Replacement copies can be requested from the U.S. Department of State's Vital Records office. We provide the instructions and details you need so the replacement can be used for apostille and overseas use.

How long does a CRODA apostille take?

Because CRODAs are federal records, they are authenticated through the U.S. Department of State rather than a state Secretary of State. Standard federal processing is currently several weeks. We confirm current timelines and rush options before you commit.

Do I need consular legalization in addition to the apostille?

Only if the country where the CRODA will be used is NOT a member of the Hague Apostille Convention. For Hague countries, the apostille alone is sufficient. For non-Hague countries, federal authentication is followed by consular legalization at that country's embassy or consulate in the U.S.

Can you help if I am already overseas?

Yes. Many families are already abroad when they realize they need additional federal authentication. We coordinate ordering, apostille, and return shipping so you do not have to travel back to the United States.
