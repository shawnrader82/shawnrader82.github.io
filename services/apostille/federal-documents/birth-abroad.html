What this page covers

CRBA apostille, end to end.

When a child is born outside the United States to U.S. citizen parent(s), the U.S. embassy or consulate may issue a Consular Report of Birth Abroad — most often on Form FS-240 — to document the child's U.S. citizenship. Foreign authorities frequently require the CRBA to be apostilled or authenticated before they will update local birth registries, issue residence permits, enroll a child in school, or process family immigration. Because the CRBA is a federal record, it is authenticated by the U.S. Department of State, not a state Secretary of State.

Common reasons people need this

When a CRBA apostille is required.

Dual citizenship & family registration

Many countries require an apostilled CRBA before they will add a child born abroad to local birth records, issue a domestic birth certificate, or recognize dual nationality — especially in jurisdictions that grant citizenship by descent.

Residence permits & family visas

Foreign immigration authorities often ask for an apostilled CRBA to confirm a child's identity and relationship to U.S. citizen parents when issuing residence permits, long-term visas, or family-reunification documents.

Foreign citizenship & passport applications

Some foreign citizenship and passport offices accept a U.S. CRBA as proof of birth but still require an apostille or authentication to verify that the document was genuinely issued by the U.S. government.

School enrollment & benefits abroad

Schools, health systems, and benefits agencies in other countries may request an apostilled CRBA to document a child's age, identity, and U.S. citizenship when registering for services or programs.

What will and won't be accepted

Accepted & rejected CRBA copies.

Will be accepted

  • Original FS-240 Consular Report of Birth Abroad issued by a U.S. embassy or consulate
  • Certified replacement copies issued by the U.S. Department of State Vital Records office
  • FS-545 or DS-1350 records (legacy CRBA forms) where the destination country still accepts them
  • Amended or corrected CRBAs reissued by the U.S. Department of State

Will be rejected

  • Photocopies, scans, or notarized copies of a CRBA (apostille goes on the federal certified copy itself)
  • Foreign hospital birth records or local birth certificates issued abroad
  • Damaged, laminated, or altered CRBAs with broken seals
  • State-issued birth certificates (those go through the state Secretary of State, not federal)

Realistic timelines

CRBA apostille processing time.

Hague Apostille countries

  • Federal apostille — issued by the U.S. Department of State Office of Authentications.
  • Standard processing — typically several weeks; current State Department times fluctuate.
  • Replacement CRBA — add 4–8 weeks if a new certified copy must be ordered from the Department of State first.

Non-Hague countries

  • Federal authentication at the U.S. Department of State (same step as apostille).
  • Consular legalization — add 2–6 weeks at the destination country's embassy or consulate in the U.S.
  • We coordinate the full chain so you do not have to chase multiple offices.

Before you contact us

Have these ready for a clear CRBA plan.

CRBA apostille checklist

  • Country where the CRBA will be used
  • Whether you have the original FS-240 in hand
  • Type of process abroad (residency, school, citizenship registration, etc.)
  • Names exactly as they appear on supporting IDs and the CRBA
  • Hard deadlines (visa appointment, consulate appointment, school registration)
  • Whether the destination country is in the Hague Apostille Convention

Good to know

  • Federal, not state: CRBAs are authenticated by the U.S. Department of State, not California or another state Secretary of State.
  • Order multiple copies: if more than one foreign authority needs an apostilled CRBA, order extra certified copies up front to avoid rework.
  • Translation: the apostille does not translate the document. Certified translation, if required, happens after apostille.

CRBA apostille FAQ

Frequently asked questions.

Can I apostille a photocopy or scan of the CRBA?

Foreign authorities generally require an original or certified copy issued by the U.S. Department of State, not a photocopy. We help you determine whether your current CRBA is suitable or whether you should request a new certified copy specifically for apostille.

What if I lost the original Consular Report of Birth Abroad?

Replacement copies can be requested from the U.S. Department of State's Vital Records office. We provide the instructions and details you need so the replacement can be used for apostille and overseas registration.

Does a CRBA have to be notarized?

A Consular Report of Birth Abroad is a federal record and normally does not require notarization. It is authenticated and apostilled through federal channels based on the embedded signatures and seals on the document itself.

Is a CRBA the same as a state-issued birth certificate?

A CRBA serves a similar purpose to a U.S. birth certificate but is issued by the U.S. Department of State rather than a state or county. Some foreign authorities accept it on its own, while others also request local birth registration or additional documents. For state-issued birth certificates, see our birth certificate apostille page.

How long does a CRBA apostille take?

Because CRBAs are federal records, they are authenticated through the U.S. Department of State rather than a state Secretary of State. Standard federal processing is currently several weeks. We confirm current timelines and rush options before you commit.

What if my destination country is not part of the Hague Apostille Convention?

Non-Hague countries require federal authentication followed by consular legalization instead of an apostille. We explain the steps for your specific country so you know whether you need an apostille, additional certificates, or full consular processing.
