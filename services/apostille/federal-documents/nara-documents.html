What this page covers

NARA & federal archive apostille, end to end.

The National Archives and Records Administration (NARA) holds historical federal records — military service files, federal pension records, immigration and naturalization records, ship passenger lists, federal census records, and other certified historical documents. Foreign governments frequently require apostilled NARA records for citizenship-by-descent applications (especially Italy, Ireland, Germany, Poland, Mexico, and Eastern Europe), foreign estate matters tied to U.S. ancestry, and international academic or genealogical research projects.

Common reasons people need this

When a NARA apostille is required.

Citizenship by descent & jure sanguinis

Italy (jure sanguinis), Ireland (Foreign Births Register), Germany, Poland, Mexico, and many other countries require apostilled NARA records — naturalization files, immigration manifests, military records — to establish ancestral lineage.

Military service & pension records abroad

Foreign pension agencies, veterans' organizations, and military attaché offices may request apostilled NARA military service records for surviving family members applying for benefits or recognition abroad.

Foreign estate & genealogy matters

International estate cases that depend on documenting U.S. ancestry — heir-tracing, lineage verification, inherited property abroad — often require apostilled NARA records as primary-source evidence.

Academic & institutional research

Universities, archives, and research institutions abroad sometimes request apostilled certified copies of NARA records when integrating U.S. historical documents into formal research projects or publications.

How it works

Three steps from archive record to apostille.

1

Identify and request the right NARA record

We help you identify which NARA branch holds the record you need (St. Louis NPRC for military, Washington DC for naturalizations, regional branches for census and immigration). We tell you exactly what to request so the resulting certified copy is eligible for apostille.

2

NARA issues certified copies with archival seal

NARA produces certified copies with the official archival seal and signature of the appropriate certifying officer. Standard photocopies or downloaded PDFs without the seal are not apostille-eligible.

3

We apostille and return

We route the certified NARA copies through the U.S. Department of State for federal apostille (or authentication plus consular legalization for non-Hague countries), track progress, and return the completed document by secure mail or courier — anywhere in the world.

What will and won't be accepted

Accepted & rejected NARA records.

Will be accepted

  • Certified copies issued by the National Archives with archival seal and certifying officer signature
  • Military service records (DD-214, OMPF) certified by the National Personnel Records Center (NPRC)
  • Naturalization records certified by NARA from federal naturalization court files
  • Immigration manifests and ship passenger lists certified by NARA
  • Federal pension and census records certified by NARA or the appropriate federal archive

Will be rejected

  • Self-printed photocopies, downloaded PDFs, or Ancestry.com / FamilySearch.org images without NARA certification
  • Records from state archives or local historical societies (handled separately, not federal)
  • Records older than NARA holdings (very old documents may live with the Library of Congress or state archives instead)
  • Certified copies without an intact archival seal or proper certifying signature

Realistic timelines

NARA & federal archive apostille processing time.

Hague Apostille countries

  • NARA certified copies — 6–12 weeks is typical, longer for high-demand branches like NPRC.
  • Federal apostille — typically several weeks at the U.S. Department of State after certification.
  • Full timeline — plan 3–6 months end-to-end for most citizenship-by-descent cases.

Non-Hague countries

  • Federal authentication at the U.S. Department of State after NARA certification.
  • Consular legalization — add 2–6 weeks at the destination country's embassy or consulate.
  • We coordinate the full chain across NARA, State, and consulate so you don't have to.

Good to know

  • Certified, not just printed: only NARA-certified copies with the archival seal are apostille-eligible. Genealogy site images are not enough.
  • Order multiple copies: citizenship-by-descent applications often need several apostilled originals for multiple generations; order extras up front.
  • Translation: the apostille does not translate the document. Certified translation, if required, happens after apostille.

NARA apostille FAQ

Frequently asked questions.

Can you apostille an Ancestry.com or FamilySearch image?

No. Genealogy sites provide images but not certified copies. Foreign governments require a certified copy issued directly by NARA with the official archival seal and a certifying officer's signature. We can help you identify which NARA branch to request from.

How do I get a NARA certified copy of a military record?

Military service records are held at the National Personnel Records Center (NPRC) in St. Louis. You can request them through eVetRecs or by mail. We can help you identify exactly what to request so the certified copy is eligible for apostille.

Which countries accept NARA apostilles for citizenship by descent?

All major jure sanguinis / citizenship-by-descent programs accept apostilled NARA records as primary-source evidence — Italy, Ireland, Germany, Poland, Mexico, Spain, Portugal, and others. Each consulate has its own specific record list; we help match your case.

How long does the full process take?

Realistic end-to-end planning is 3–6 months. NARA certification alone often takes 6–12 weeks (longer for NPRC military records), and federal apostille adds several more weeks. We provide a case-specific estimate up front.

Do I need consular legalization in addition to the apostille?

Only if your destination country is NOT a member of the Hague Apostille Convention. For Hague countries (which includes nearly all major citizenship-by-descent destinations), the apostille alone is sufficient.

Can I order multiple certified copies at once?

Yes — and you should, if multiple generations or family members need apostilled records. Ordering extras up front is much cheaper than re-requesting later.
