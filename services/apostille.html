Vital records — birth, marriage, death

  • Use certified copies issued by the correct vital records office (County Recorder or state Department of Public Health), not photocopies or hospital souvenirs.
  • Check whether the destination country requires a recently issued certificate — for some immigration and dual-citizenship cases, older records may need to be reissued.
  • Make sure the registrar’s original signature and official seal are clearly visible so the Secretary of State can verify the record.
  • Order enough certified copies if you will use the same record for more than one country or process, because each apostille is attached permanently to one document.
Works for apostille

Certified birth, marriage, or death certificate from the county or state vital records office, with original registrar signature and raised or printed seal.

Will be rejected

Hospital souvenirs, photocopies, scans printed at home, or records with missing signatures or seals cannot be apostilled and must be replaced with certified copies.

For vital records, we confirm that your certificates are the right type, issued by the right office, and acceptable for your destination country before you spend time and money sending them for apostille.

Court, divorce, and adoption records

  • Use certified copies issued by the court clerk or appropriate registrar, with the original court seal and clerk’s signature visible — plain file-stamped copies are often not enough.
  • Confirm that the full judgment or decree is included, not just a summary page, especially for divorce and adoption orders that foreign courts review closely.
  • Avoid altered, stapled-over, or partially scanned sets — foreign authorities frequently require the complete, intact packet as issued by the court.
  • For international adoptions or complex custody matters, confirm with your agency or attorney which specific orders and supporting documents must be apostilled so nothing is missed.
Works for apostille

Certified court copies of divorce decrees, custody orders, or adoption judgments issued by the clerk, with original seal and handwritten or stamped signature on the certification.

Will be rejected

Online printouts from court portals, photocopies without a court seal, or partial packets where pages are missing or separated from the certification page.

For court and adoption records, we review your copies first and let you know if you need certified court copies or additional notarized documents before submitting anything for apostille or legalization.

Business & corporate documents

  • Confirm which documents must come directly from the state (for example, certificates of good standing or certified copies of articles) and which can be notarized internal documents (such as resolutions or powers of attorney).

Official certificate of good standing or certified articles from the Secretary of State, or a properly notarized board resolution or corporate power of attorney naming who can sign abroad.

Powers of attorney & notarized letters

  • Have your power of attorney or affidavit signed in front of a qualified notary public, using proper acknowledgment or jurat wording, with a clear seal and the notary’s signature and commission details.
  • Make sure every name, address, and ID number in the document is correct and matches your identification — many countries will reject documents with cross-outs, initials, or inconsistent spelling.
  • For country-specific forms (such as special powers of attorney or consent letters required overseas), use the exact template or wording requested by the receiving attorney, consulate, or agency before notarization.
  • Avoid scanning, reprinting, or re-notarizing copies of old documents — the apostille must be attached to the original notarized document with the notary’s live ink signature and seal.
Works for apostille

Original power of attorney or notarized letter signed in front of a notary, with complete notary certificate, clear seal, correct venue (state and county), and no corrections or missing initials.

Will be rejected

Unsigned drafts, documents with incomplete or incorrect notarization, copies without the original notary seal, or letters that were signed abroad without following local notary and apostille rules.

For powers of attorney and consent letters, we help you use the right format, ensure the notarization meets apostille standards, and confirm any extra steps needed for your destination country before you send documents out.

Real-world timelines

How long does this really take?

Every case is different, but here is a realistic look at how long common apostille and legalization paths can take once your documents are signed and notarized.

State apostille only

  • Most states issue apostilles in about 3–10 business days by mail, with some offering walk-in or expedited options where available.
  • Busy states or peak seasons can stretch this to a few weeks, especially if you are mailing documents instead of appearing in person.
  • Ordering new vital records first can add extra days or weeks before the apostille process even starts.

Good fit for Hague countries where your documents only need a state-level apostille, such as Mexico, Spain, or the Philippines.

Federal or multi-step paths

  • U.S. Department of State apostille/authentication is often measured in weeks from when your packet is received, with recent estimates in the 4–8 week range by mail.
  • Non-Hague routes that require state authentication, a federal step, and a foreign embassy or consulate can easily take several weeks end-to-end.
  • Embassy and consulate processing alone may add another 1–3 weeks, depending on the country, volume, and whether you apply in person or by mail.

Countries like Thailand, Taiwan, and Cuba follow these longer consular-legalization paths because they do not accept apostilles.

What can speed things up

  • Knowing exactly which state or federal office needs to handle each document, and whether your destination is Hague or non-Hague.
  • Using in-person submission or a professional courier service where the issuing office allows it.
  • Having all your documents reviewed and corrected before you submit, so nothing is rejected and sent back.

What can slow things down

  • Missing or outdated documents that must be reissued before the apostille process can begin.
  • Notarization errors that cause the Secretary of State to reject a document and return it for correction.
  • Waiting until the last minute before a consular appointment, travel, or filing deadline.

Apostille & legalization FAQ

What is an apostille?

An apostille is a certificate issued by a designated government authority (usually the Secretary of State for state documents, or the U.S. Department of State for federal documents) that verifies the signature and seal on a public document so it will be accepted by officials in another country that is part of the Hague Apostille Convention.

What is the difference between an apostille and consular legalization?

An apostille is a single-step verification used in countries that are members of the Hague Convention. Consular legalization is a multi-step process used for countries that are not Hague members such as Taiwan, Thailand, and Cuba — it typically requires state or federal authentication followed by legalization at the destination country’s embassy or consulate.

State apostilles in California typically take 3–10 business days by mail. Federal apostilles through the U.S. Department of State can take 4–8 weeks. Consular legalization adds additional time depending on the embassy. We give you a realistic timeline estimate before you commit. For urgent cases, ask about our same-day rush options.

Can you apostille documents from other states, not just California?

Yes. We handle documents issued by any U.S. state. Each document must be apostilled by the Secretary of State in the state where it was issued or notarized, and we coordinate that process for you regardless of the state.

Do I need to get my document notarized before getting an apostille?

It depends on the document. Vital records (birth, marriage, death certificates) and court-certified copies already carry an official signature and seal, so they go directly to the Secretary of State. Private documents like powers of attorney, affidavits, and corporate resolutions must be notarized first, because the apostille verifies the notary’s commission.

What if my document gets rejected?

Rejections usually happen because of incorrect notarization, missing seals, outdated records, or submitting to the wrong office. We review every document before submission specifically to prevent this. If a problem comes up, we will explain the fix and help you correct it as quickly as possible.

Do I also need a translation?

Many countries require a certified or sworn translation of your document in addition to the apostille. Some countries like Spain require a specific type of sworn translator.
