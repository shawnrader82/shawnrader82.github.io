Understanding The Document

What Is A Bill Of Sale?

A bill of sale is a written document that records the transfer of ownership of property from a seller to a buyer. It includes the names of both parties, a description of the item, the sale price, and the date of the transaction. It serves as a receipt and proof of the agreement for both sides.

Common Types Of Bills Of Sale

  • Vehicle bill of sale - cars, trucks, motorcycles, and RVs sold between private parties.
  • Boat and watercraft bill of sale - sailboats, motorboats, jet skis, and other vessels.
  • Equipment and machinery bill of sale - tools, construction equipment, and commercial machinery.
  • General personal property bill of sale - furniture, electronics, jewelry, art, livestock, and other valuables.
  • Business asset bill of sale - sale of business equipment, inventory, or an entire business.

Why Notarize A Bill Of Sale?

  • Protects the buyer by proving the seller voluntarily transferred ownership.
  • Protects the seller by proving the buyer agreed to the terms and price.
  • Reduces the risk of disputes about what was agreed and when.
  • Some out-of-state DMVs require a notarized bill of sale to register a vehicle purchased in California.
  • Lenders and insurance companies may require notarization for high-value transactions.
  • A notarized bill of sale carries more weight if the transaction is ever disputed in court.
California Requirements

Bill Of Sale Notarization Rules In California

California does not require notarization for most private-party bills of sale. However, there are situations where notarization is either required or strongly recommended.

When Notarization May Be Required

  • The buyer is registering the vehicle in another state that requires a notarized bill of sale.
  • A lien exists on the vehicle and DMV forms like the REG 227 or REG 166 are involved, which may need notarization.
  • A financial institution or lender requires notarization before releasing funds or processing a loan.
  • The transaction involves a business sale or high-value property where both parties want added legal protection.

When Notarization Is Recommended

  • Any private-party vehicle sale where the buyer and seller do not know each other well.
  • Sales involving older or classic vehicles where title history may be unclear.
  • High-value personal property transactions like equipment, art, or jewelry.
  • Any time either party wants proof that the other person signed willingly and showed proper ID.

Note: For standard California vehicle transfers, the DMV accepts the bill of sale section on the REG 262 form or the standalone REG 135 form. Neither requires notarization by default. However, having a notarized bill of sale adds a layer of protection beyond the minimum requirements.

What Your Document Should Include

Contents Of A Proper Bill Of Sale

Required Information

  • Full legal names and contact information for both buyer and seller.
  • Description of the item being sold, including make, model, year, and color.
  • VIN (Vehicle Identification Number) for vehicles, or HIN (Hull Identification Number) for boats.
  • Serial numbers for equipment or machinery if applicable.
  • Sale price and payment method.
  • Date of the sale.
  • Signatures of both buyer and seller.

Recommended Additions

  • A statement that the seller holds clear title with no undisclosed liens.
  • An as-is clause if the item is sold without warranty.
  • Current mileage or engine hours for vehicles and equipment.
  • Condition notes describing any known issues or defects.
  • Odometer disclosure statement if required by the transaction type.
  • Space for the notarial certificate to be attached or printed.
Notarization Details

How A Bill Of Sale Gets Notarized

Who Needs To Be Present

  • Both the buyer and the seller should be present with valid, current government issued photo ID.
  • If only one party's signature needs notarization, only that person is required.
  • If a representative is signing on behalf of one party using a power of attorney, they must bring the original POA and their own ID.

At Your Appointment

  1. The mobile notary arrives at the agreed location and reviews each signer's ID.
  2. Both parties review the bill of sale and confirm the details are correct.
  3. The notary determines whether an acknowledgment or jurat is needed based on the document language.
  4. Both parties sign in front of the notary.
  5. The notary completes the notarial certificate, applies their seal, and records the notarization in their journal.

Frequently Asked Questions

Bill Of Sale Notarization FAQ

Does a bill of sale need to be notarized in California?

California does not require notarization for most private vehicle or boat bills of sale. However, notarization is strongly recommended because it protects both the buyer and seller. Some lenders, out-of-state DMVs, and private parties may require or prefer a notarized copy before completing the transaction.

When is a notarized bill of sale required?

A notarized bill of sale may be required when the buyer is registering in another state that requires it, when a lender or financial institution requests it, when replacement title forms with liens are involved, or when the transaction is high value and both parties want extra protection.

What should a bill of sale include?

A proper bill of sale should include the full legal names and contact information of the buyer and seller, a description of the item with make, model, year, and VIN or serial number, the sale price and payment method, the date of sale, and signatures of both parties.

What type of notarization does a bill of sale require?

A bill of sale typically uses an acknowledgment, where both parties confirm their identities and that they are signing voluntarily. If the document includes sworn statements about the condition or history of the item, a jurat may be used instead.
