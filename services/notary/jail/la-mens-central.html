Before your appointment

What to have ready for a jail notary visit.

✓

Valid photo ID for signers

Every signer needs a current government-issued photo ID. For inmates, jail-issued ID cards are generally acceptable. Confirm the inmate’s current ID status before scheduling — if their ID was booked into property, it may need to be retrieved first.

✓

Complete documents ready to sign

Bring all pages of the document — not just the signature page. Documents should be fully prepared and ready to sign. Missing pages or incomplete forms will require a return visit. We can advise on preparation before we schedule.

✓

Inmate name & booking number

We need the inmate’s full legal name and booking number to confirm their location within the facility. You can typically find the booking number through the county sheriff’s online inmate search or by calling the facility directly.

Mobile Notary FAQ — Men's Central Jail

Frequently asked questions.

How do I find someone's booking number at Men's Central Jail?

You can search the LA County Sheriff's Inmate Information Center online at app5.lasd.org/iic using the inmate's name and date of birth. The booking number helps confirm their location and coordinate the visit.

What if the inmate was just transferred to MCJ?

After a transfer, it can take 24–48 hours for an inmate to be fully processed and available for professional visits. We recommend confirming their status before scheduling if there has been a recent transfer.

Can family members bring documents to the jail notary appointment?

No — professional notary visits are separate from regular family visitation. You will need to get documents to us before the appointment, and we will bring them into the facility.

What ID does the inmate need for notarization at MCJ?

MCJ maintains booking records with photos that can be used for identification purposes. If the inmate has a valid California ID, driver's license, or passport on file, that can also be used.

How long does a Men's Central Jail notary appointment take?

Plan for 1–2 hours total including travel, security screening, wait time, and the actual signing. The notarization itself is quick, but jail procedures add time.

What if the inmate refuses to sign the documents?

A notary cannot force anyone to sign a document. If the inmate refuses or is unable to sign, the notarization cannot be completed. You may still be responsible for a trip fee in this situation.
