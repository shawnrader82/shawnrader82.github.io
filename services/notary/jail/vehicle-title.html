DMV forms we notarize

Which California DMV form does your transaction require?

REG 227

Application for Duplicate or Transfer of Title

The primary form for transferring vehicle ownership. Required when the original title is lost, damaged, or unavailable. The seller's signature must be notarized. The buyer submits REG 227 to DMV to obtain a new title in their name.

REG 138

Notice of Transfer and Release of Liability

Filed by the seller after the vehicle is sold to release ongoing liability for parking tickets, accidents, and other incidents. Submitted online or by mail to DMV. Protects the seller once the vehicle leaves their possession.

Bill of Sale

Vehicle Bill of Sale

Documents the sale price, buyer and seller information, and vehicle details. Not required by California DMV but highly recommended for tax, liability, and dispute-prevention purposes. We notarize the seller's signature upon request.

Lien Release

Lien Release

Required if the vehicle has an outstanding loan. The lienholder (bank or finance company) must provide a signed lien release before the title can be transferred free and clear. We notarize the release if the lienholder requires it.

Common scenarios

When families call us for an inmate vehicle title notarization.

Selling the Vehicle to Pay Legal Fees

The inmate needs to sell their car to fund attorney retainer fees or post bail. The buyer requires a notarized title transfer before completing the purchase, and the inmate must sign in front of a notary while in custody.

Transferring Ownership to a Family Member

The inmate wants to transfer the vehicle to a spouse, parent, or sibling to keep it insured and operable during incarceration. A notarized title transfer prevents the vehicle from sitting uninsured and incurring registration penalties.

Lost or Unavailable Title

The original title is at home, in storage, or lost. California DMV allows a notarized REG 227 to substitute for the lost title in the transfer process. We notarize the REG 227 at the facility; the buyer or family member submits it to DMV.

POA Needed First

The inmate cannot travel to DMV and wants to grant a family member authority to complete the transfer. We notarize both the power of attorney and the title documents in a single jail visit — one clearance, two documents completed.

How it works

Five steps from your call to notarized vehicle title documents.

1

Confirm the Forms and Facility

Call or text us with the facility name, inmate full name, and which DMV forms need to be notarized. If possible, text photos of the completed forms so we can confirm the correct notarial act before we travel. Confirm with DMV or your attorney which forms are required for your specific transaction before scheduling.

2

Clearance Request

We submit a professional visit clearance request with the facility. Most LA County jails process clearance within 24–48 hours. If a vehicle sale has a hard deadline, let us know immediately — we communicate urgency and work to expedite where facility scheduling permits.

3

Identity Verification at the Facility

On arrival, we clear security and verify the inmate's identity using a government-issued photo ID or facility booking records. California law requires satisfactory identification before any notarial act — vehicle title notarizations are no exception.

4

Document Review and Signing

We review the DMV forms for completeness — all vehicle information, buyer/seller details, and odometer disclosure filled in. The inmate then signs all required documents in front of us. The signature must occur in the notary's presence — do not sign before we arrive.

5

Notarization and Delivery

We complete the notarial acknowledgment certificates, apply our official seal, and record all acts in our notary journal. The notarized documents are returned to you for submission to DMV, the buyer, or escrow — per your instructions.

Vehicle title transfer FAQ

Frequently asked questions about inmate vehicle title notarization.

What DMV forms does an inmate need to transfer a vehicle?

The most common forms are REG 227 (Application for Duplicate or Transfer of Title, signed by the seller), REG 138 (Notice of Transfer and Release of Liability), and a Bill of Sale. If there is a lien, a signed lien release from the lienholder is also required. Confirm with DMV or your attorney which forms apply to your specific situation before scheduling.

Does a vehicle title transfer require notarization in California?

California DMV requires notarization on REG 227 when the certificate of title is not available. Even when the title is present, notarization is strongly recommended when the seller is incarcerated — it eliminates disputes about the validity of the signing and is often required by buyers, dealerships, and financial institutions processing the transfer.

Can an inmate sign a vehicle title transfer while in jail?

Yes. An incarcerated vehicle owner retains full legal authority to sell or transfer their vehicle. We travel to the facility, verify identity, and notarize the required DMV forms so the transfer can proceed while the inmate is in custody.

What if the inmate does not have the physical title?

If the original title is lost or unavailable, the inmate must complete DMV REG 227. The notarized REG 227 substitutes for the lost title. We notarize it at the facility; the buyer then submits it to DMV with the other required documents.

Can a family member sign on behalf of an incarcerated vehicle owner?

Yes — if the inmate first grants a notarized power of attorney authorizing the agent to transfer the vehicle. Without a valid notarized POA, the registered owner must personally sign the title documents. We can notarize both the POA and the title documents in the same jail visit.

How long does scheduling take for a vehicle title notarization at jail?

Most LA County jails clear professional visits within 24–48 hours. Out-of-county facilities vary. Have all DMV forms fully completed before scheduling — we confirm which forms are needed when you call.
