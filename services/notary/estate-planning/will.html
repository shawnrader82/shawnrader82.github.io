What we notarize and witness

Will documents we handle.

The will itself takes two qualifying witnesses; the attached self-proving affidavit takes a notary. We coordinate both so the will is properly executed the first time.

  • Last Will & Testament Signed by the testator in front of two qualifying adult witnesses.
  • Self-Proving Affidavit Attached to the will — testator and both witnesses sign under oath in front of a notary.
  • Codicil An amendment to an existing will. Same witness + notary formalities as the original will.
  • Holographic Will Handwritten will signed by the testator. No witnesses required but optionally notarized for evidence.
  • Pour-Over Will Companion to a revocable living trust — directs remaining assets into the trust at death.
  • Statutory Will California's pre-printed fill-in-the-blank will form. Same witness + notary requirements.
  • Living Will Note: in California this is usually called an Advance Healthcare Directive — see the AHCD page.
  • HIPAA Release Often signed alongside a will or advance directive. Witnessed or notarized.

Your attorney or self-help service may include other supporting documents (asset inventory, letter of instruction, beneficiary designations). We adapt to whatever the package contains.

Will signing types

What kind of will signing do you need?

Most will signings fall into one of three patterns. Here's what each requires, how long it takes, and what we bring.

Standard will signing

You have a typed will from an attorney or online service. The testator signs in front of two qualifying witnesses; then everyone signs the self-proving affidavit in front of the notary.

  • Typical document size: 8–25 pages
  • Signing time: 20–35 minutes
  • Two qualifying witnesses + notary required
  • Codicils and pour-over wills handled the same way

Self-proving affidavit only

Your will was already signed and witnessed, but the self-proving affidavit wasn't notarized at the time. We notarize the affidavit so the will can be admitted to probate without the witnesses being called to court.

  • Just the affidavit page — usually 1–2 pages
  • Signing time: 10–15 minutes
  • Original witnesses must also re-sign in front of the notary
  • Saves significant time and cost in probate

With witnesses we bring

You don't have two disinterested adult witnesses available — common for solo signers or signers in care facilities. We arrange two qualifying witnesses to meet us at your location.

  • Additional fee for witness coordination
  • Witnesses screened for disinterest and adult status
  • Works for hospital, hospice, SNF, and home signings
  • Confirm at booking so we can scout availability

Will signing FAQ

Common questions about will signings.

Does a will need to be notarized in California?

The will itself does not require notarization in California — it requires two qualifying witnesses. However, the attached self-proving affidavit (which streamlines probate by letting the will be admitted without calling witnesses to court) does require notarization. Most modern wills include a self-proving affidavit, which is why a notary is part of almost every will signing.

What is a self-proving affidavit?

A self-proving affidavit is a separate page (or section) attached to the will where the testator and witnesses swear under oath, in front of a notary, that the will was properly executed. With this affidavit, the will can be admitted to probate without the court needing to track down and depose the original witnesses — a significant time and cost savings for your family.

Who qualifies as a witness to a will in California?

Under California Probate Code §6112, a witness must be an adult (18+), of sound mind, and present at the same time as the testator and the other witness. Anyone who would inherit under the will (a beneficiary) should NOT serve as a witness — their gift can be reduced or voided. The notary cannot be a witness either.

Can you bring witnesses to my will signing?

Yes. For an additional fee we can coordinate two disinterested adult witnesses to meet us at your signing location — your home, attorney's office, hospital, hospice, or skilled nursing facility. Let us know when you schedule whether you need us to bring witnesses or whether you'll have neighbors, friends, or facility staff available.

Can you come to a hospital or hospice for a will signing?

Yes — this is one of our most frequent requests. We routinely sign wills bedside at hospitals, hospices, and skilled nursing facilities across Los Angeles. The testator must be alert, aware of what they are signing, and able to communicate willingness. We screen for capacity on arrival and document the signing carefully.

What ID does the testator need?

For the notarized self-proving affidavit, California law requires a current, unexpired government-issued photo ID — driver license, state ID, U.S. passport, or foreign passport. For bedside signings where the signer has no ID, two credible witnesses (with their own valid IDs, who personally know the signer) can be used. The two witnesses to the will itself do not need to present ID to us, but they should be ready to identify themselves.

Can you draft my will or recommend what to put in it?

No — we are notaries and signing-services agents, not attorneys. We cannot draft your will, choose your executor, decide who inherits what, or explain legal effect. We notarize the self-proving affidavit and arrange/witness the signing of a will that you, your attorney, or an online service has already prepared.
