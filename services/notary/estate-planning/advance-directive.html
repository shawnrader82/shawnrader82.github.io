What we notarize

AHCD documents we handle.

California recognizes several advance care documents. We notarize whatever your attorney, hospital social worker, or care planner provides — these are the ones we see most often.

  • California Statutory AHCD The official Probate Code §4701 fill-in-the-blank form — free from the California AG.
  • Attorney-Drafted AHCD Custom advance directive prepared as part of a comprehensive estate plan.
  • HIPAA Release Authorizes named individuals to receive medical information — often signed alongside AHCD.
  • Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare Older terminology — California folded this into the modern AHCD.
  • Mental Health Advance Directive Specific instructions for psychiatric treatment — same execution rules.
  • Anatomical Gift / Organ Donation Statement of intent regarding organ and tissue donation.
  • Revocation of Prior AHCD Cancels a previously executed advance directive — must be properly delivered.
  • POLST (Physician Orders) Note: POLST is a physician-signed medical order — we don't notarize these.

Your attorney, hospital, or self-help service may include other supporting documents (out-of-hospital DNR, dementia provisions, religious directives). We adapt to whatever the package contains.

AHCD signing types

What kind of AHCD signing do you need?

Most AHCD signings fall into one of three patterns. Here's what each requires, how long it takes, and what we look for.

Standard AHCD signing

You have a completed AHCD (statutory form or attorney-drafted) and you're at home, the attorney's office, or a planned signing location. The principal signs in front of the notary — no witnesses required when notarized.

  • Typical document size: 6–12 pages
  • Signing time: 15–25 minutes
  • Principal must demonstrate awareness
  • Healthcare agent may attend but cannot coach

AHCD + HIPAA release

Pairs an AHCD with a separate HIPAA authorization so the healthcare agent (and named family members) can receive protected health information from doctors and hospitals before the AHCD itself is triggered.

  • Two notarizations — one per document
  • Signing time: 25–40 minutes
  • Helpful for proactive medical coordination
  • Often requested together by estate planning attorneys

Bedside hospital / hospice signing

Urgent AHCD signing for a patient currently admitted to a hospital, hospice, or skilled nursing facility. Capacity, identification, and timing are the main considerations — we are experienced with all three.

  • Capacity screened carefully on arrival
  • Credible witnesses accepted if principal has no ID
  • Signing time: 20–40 minutes
  • SNF residents: California has special witness rules — notarization is the simpler path

AHCD signing FAQ

Common questions about Advance Healthcare Directive signings.

Does an Advance Healthcare Directive need to be notarized in California?

Under California Probate Code §4673, an AHCD is valid if it is either notarized OR signed by two qualifying witnesses. Either method makes the document legally binding. Many hospitals and physicians prefer notarized AHCDs because they remove any ambiguity about identity and capacity, but the witnessed route is equally valid under California law.

Who can be a witness to an AHCD in California?

California has strict rules: a witness must be an adult, cannot be the person's healthcare agent, cannot be the person's healthcare provider or an employee of the provider, and cannot be an operator/employee of the residential care facility where the person lives. If the person lives in a skilled nursing facility, one of the witnesses must be a patient advocate or ombudsman. These rules make notarization the easier path for many patients.

What's the difference between an AHCD, a living will, and a POLST?

An Advance Healthcare Directive is the California document that names a healthcare agent (durable power of attorney for healthcare) AND records your treatment wishes — it replaces the older "living will" terminology. A POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) is a separate medical order signed by a doctor for seriously ill patients; it is not notarized and follows different procedures. We notarize AHCDs; POLST forms are physician-signed only.

Can you come to a hospital or hospice for an AHCD signing?

Yes — this is one of our most common requests. We routinely sign AHCDs bedside at hospitals, hospices, and skilled nursing facilities across Los Angeles. The principal must be alert, aware of what they are signing, and able to communicate willingness. We screen for capacity on arrival and document the signing carefully so the AHCD will hold up to medical and family scrutiny.

What ID does the principal need?

California law requires a current, unexpired government-issued photo ID — driver license, state ID, U.S. passport, or foreign passport with photo. For bedside signings where the principal has no ID, two credible witnesses (with their own valid IDs, who personally know the principal) can identify them under oath. The healthcare agent named in the AHCD cannot be a credible witness.

Can the healthcare agent be present at the signing?

Yes, but the agent should not coach, prompt, or pressure the principal. The notary must independently verify that the principal understands what they are signing and is acting voluntarily. If we observe coercion or capacity concerns, we will decline the signing — this protects the principal and protects the AHCD from being challenged later.

Can you draft my AHCD or advise on what to put in it?

No — we are notaries and signing-services agents, not attorneys or healthcare providers. We cannot draft your AHCD, choose your healthcare agent, decide treatment preferences, or explain medical or legal effect. We notarize an AHCD that you, your attorney, or a healthcare provider has already prepared. California's official statutory AHCD form is free and available from the California Attorney General's office.
