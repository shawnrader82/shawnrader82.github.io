How bedside notarization works

Five steps from your call to a notarized document.

Hospital notary signings have more moving parts than a standard mobile notary visit. Here is exactly what happens from your first call to the completed notarization.

Step 1 — Tell us about the patient and document

  • Call or text (213) 933-2507 with the hospital name, patient room or unit, and what needs to be notarized.
  • If you have a document ready, text us a photo so we can confirm the notarial act required (acknowledgment vs. jurat) before we travel.
  • If you need a document created on-site, let us know — we will bring laptop and printer.

Step 2 — Signer verification and capacity check

  • On arrival, we check in with hospital staff and confirm visitor access to the patient floor or ICU.
  • We verify the patient's identity using a current government-issued photo ID (or credible identifying witnesses if no ID is available).
  • We conduct a brief capacity assessment — asking the patient to state their name, the date, and what the document does — as required by California law.

Step 3 — Document review at the bedside

  • We review the document for completeness: all blanks filled, no blank pages after the signature line, and the correct notarial certificate attached.
  • If the document was prepared by an attorney, we follow their instructions precisely. We do not advise on content.
  • If the document must be created on-site, we generate it using our laptop and Rocket Lawyer access, review it with the patient, then print it.

Step 4 — Notarization and journal entry

  • The patient signs or makes their mark in front of us.
  • We complete the notarial certificate, apply our seal and signature, and make a journal entry as required by California Notary law.
  • If two witnesses are required (e.g., for a will or alternative AHCD execution), they must be present and sign before we complete the act.

Step 5 — Copies and follow-up

  • We can scan the completed document on-site or email a digital copy to you, the patient's attorney, or the hospital care team.
  • We advise on how many originals to retain and where to file them (with the patient's physician, hospital chart, and personal records).

Hospital notary FAQ

Questions about bedside notarization in California.

How quickly can you get to the hospital?

For urgent situations, call or text (213) 933-2507 directly. San Fernando Valley and Santa Clarita hospitals are typically 10–40 minutes from our Granada Hills base. Hollywood, Beverly Grove, Westwood, and Ventura County hospitals are typically 35–60+ minutes. We prioritize hospital signings and can often accommodate same-day requests.

Does the patient need to be mentally competent to sign?

Yes. Under California law a notary must be satisfied that the signer understands what they are signing and is acting of their own free will. A patient who is sedated, unconscious, or unable to communicate cannot be notarized. If there is any doubt, we perform a brief capacity check — asking the patient to state their name, the date, and what the document does. If capacity cannot be confirmed we cannot proceed.

What if the patient cannot hold a pen and must sign by mark?

California allows a signer to make a mark (an X or other mark) in place of a signature, provided two disinterested witnesses are present who also sign the document. If you anticipate the patient may not be able to sign conventionally, let us know in advance so we can coordinate witness arrangements.

What ID does the patient need?

California requires a valid, unexpired government-issued photo ID — driver's license, state ID card, U.S. passport, or foreign passport. If the patient does not have ID at the bedside, two credible identifying witnesses who personally know the patient and present their own IDs can serve as an alternative under California Notary law.

Does California require notarization of an Advance Healthcare Directive?

California law (Probate Code §4700 et seq.) allows an Advance Healthcare Directive to be either notarized OR witnessed by two qualifying individuals. Notarization is generally preferred in hospital settings because hospital staff who may benefit from your estate cannot serve as witnesses, and a notarized document is more broadly accepted across state lines and by institutions unfamiliar with the two-witness alternative.

Can you create documents at the hospital if we don't have them?

Yes. We bring a laptop, portable printer, and internet connection to every hospital appointment. You can use our Rocket Lawyer access to generate advance healthcare directives, powers of attorney, and other forms on-site, then sign and notarize them in the same visit. This is strictly administrative support — we are commissioned notaries, not attorneys, and do not provide legal advice.

Does POLST require a notary in California?

No. A California POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) is a physician order that requires the signature of a physician, NP, or PA plus the patient or surrogate — not a notary. Some families request notary witnessing as a precaution against family disputes. We can provide witnessing service on request but cannot make a POLST legally binding through notarization alone.

Do you serve nursing homes, rehab centers, and hospice?

Yes. We serve hospitals, skilled nursing facilities, rehabilitation centers, assisted living communities, memory care units, and hospice facilities throughout Los Angeles and Ventura County. If your facility is not listed among our hospital pages, call us to confirm coverage and pricing.
