What this page covers

POLST, DNR, and why a notary is not part of the process.

A California POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) is a standardized bright-pink medical form that translates a patient's end-of-life wishes into physician orders — directing emergency responders, nurses, and care facility staff on CPR, medical interventions, and artificial nutrition. Unlike an Advance Healthcare Directive, which is the patient's own legal document, a POLST is a physician order: it must be signed by a physician, nurse practitioner, or physician assistant plus the patient or authorized surrogate. Notarization is not part of the POLST process and does not change its legal effect. We are transparent about this because honesty matters — if you need a notarized document, we can help you execute an Advance Healthcare Directive instead.

POLST vs. Advance Healthcare Directive

Understanding when each document applies.

California POLST

  • Physician order — requires MD, NP, or PA signature
  • Covers CPR, medical interventions, and artificial nutrition
  • Has immediate force with emergency responders and care staff
  • Does NOT require notarization
  • Travels with the patient between care settings
  • Best for patients with serious illness or approaching end of life

Advance Healthcare Directive

  • Patient's legal document — signed by the patient
  • Designates a healthcare agent and documents treatment wishes
  • Requires notarization OR two qualifying witnesses
  • Guides long-term care decisions and agent authority
  • Does not have immediate force with emergency responders (unlike POLST)
  • Appropriate for any adult — not just those with serious illness

POLST FAQ

Frequently asked questions about POLST in California.

Does a California POLST form require notarization?

No. A California POLST is a physician order that requires the signatures of a licensed physician, NP, or PA plus the patient or surrogate. A notary cannot make a POLST legally binding, and notarizing it does not change its legal effect. If you need a notarized end-of-life document, the Advance Healthcare Directive is what you need — and we can notarize that at the bedside.

What is a POLST form?

A California POLST (bright pink form) is a medical order that translates a patient's wishes into immediate actionable instructions for emergency responders and care facility staff. It covers CPR preferences, medical intervention levels (full treatment, limited interventions, or comfort-focused care), and artificial nutrition preferences. Unlike an AHCD, it travels with the patient between care settings and takes immediate effect.

What is the difference between a POLST and an Advance Healthcare Directive?

An AHCD is the patient's own legal document — designating an agent and documenting general wishes — which requires notarization or witnesses. A POLST is a physician order signed by a licensed clinician. They are complementary: the AHCD guides long-term care decisions and agent authority; the POLST gives emergency responders and care staff immediate instructions. Many seriously ill patients benefit from having both.

What is a DNR and how does it relate to POLST?

A DNR (Do Not Resuscitate) is a physician's order directing staff not to perform CPR if a patient's heart or breathing stops. In California, a DNR is the CPR section of the POLST form — not a separate document. Neither requires notarization. Both require physician authorization and cannot be executed by a notary alone.

Should I have both a POLST and an Advance Healthcare Directive?

Many patients with serious illness benefit from both. The AHCD designates who speaks for you if you lose capacity and documents your broader values. The POLST gives emergency responders and care staff actionable orders without requiring them to find your AHCD or call a physician first. Your attending physician can advise on whether a POLST is appropriate for your situation.
