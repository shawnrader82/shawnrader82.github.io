How inmate notarization works

Six steps from your call to a notarized document.

Jail notarizations involve additional coordination that standard mobile notary visits do not require. Here is exactly what happens from your first call through completed notarization, including the distinction between general professional access and attorney-of-record clearance.

Step 1 — Tell us the facility and document

  • Call or text (213) 933-2507 with the facility name, inmate full name, booking number if available, and what needs to be notarized.
  • If you have the document ready, text us a photo so we can confirm the correct notarial act (acknowledgment vs. jurat) before we travel.
  • If you need guidance on which form is required, we can refer you to your attorney or the court self-help center — we cannot provide legal advice but we can confirm what form we are notarizing.

Step 2 — Clearance request and facility coordination

  • We submit a professional visit clearance request with the facility. This typically takes 24–48 hours for LA County jails and varies by facility for other counties.
  • Attorney-of-record access can sometimes expedite clearance at certain facilities. If your attorney is sponsoring the visit, let us know so we can coordinate with their office.
  • Federal facilities (such as MDC Los Angeles) require separate BOP clearance, which may take additional time. We handle all coordination and keep you updated.

Step 3 — ID verification at the facility

  • On arrival, we check in at the facility's professional visitation desk and clear security.
  • We verify the inmate's identity using a current government-issued photo ID (driver's license, state ID, passport). If no personal ID is available, many facilities allow identification through official booking records — we confirm this with the facility before scheduling.
  • California law requires satisfactory identification before any notarial act. We cannot proceed if identification cannot be established.

Step 4 — Document review

  • We review the document for completeness: all blanks filled, correct notarial certificate attached, and no unauthorized alterations.
  • The inmate must not sign before we arrive — the signature must occur in front of the notary.
  • If additional signers outside the facility must also sign (e.g., a co-owner on a vehicle title), we confirm the out-of-custody signing plan with you before the jail visit.

Step 5 — Notarization and journal entry

  • The inmate signs the document in front of us. We complete the California notarial certificate, apply our official seal and signature, and record the act in our notary journal as required by California law.
  • The inmate must be mentally competent and signing voluntarily. We cannot notarize a signature under duress or from a person who cannot demonstrate understanding of what they are signing.

Step 6 — Document delivery

  • The completed notarized document is returned to you, your attorney, escrow officer, or the court — by hand, courier, or mail, per your instructions.
  • We advise on how many originals to retain and whether certified copies or additional notarizations are needed by the receiving institution.

Jail inmate notary FAQ

Questions about inmate notarization in California.

Can an inmate legally sign documents while in custody?

Yes. Being in custody does not remove a person's legal right to sign documents. As long as the inmate is mentally competent, willing to sign voluntarily, and can be properly identified, a California notary can notarize their signature just as with any other signer. Incarceration alone does not affect legal capacity.

How long does clearance take before a notary visit?

Clearance time varies by facility. Most LA County jails require 24–48 hours notice for professional visits. Attorney-sponsored visits may clear faster at some facilities. Federal facilities such as MDC Los Angeles use a separate Bureau of Prisons clearance process that can take longer. Call us as soon as you know you need the visit — we begin the clearance process immediately.

What ID does the inmate need for a notary appointment?

Acceptable identification includes a valid driver's license, state ID card, U.S. passport, or other government-issued photo ID. Many facilities can verify the inmate's identity through official booking records when personal ID is unavailable. We confirm the identification method available at your specific facility before scheduling.

Does the notary need to go through an attorney to get access?

No. Notaries enter facilities as professional visitors, not as attorneys, and attorney-of-record clearance is not required at most county jails. However, working through an attorney of record can sometimes streamline scheduling at certain facilities. If your attorney prefers to sponsor the visit, we coordinate with their office directly.

Can family members be present during the notary appointment?

Most jail facilities do not permit family members to be present during a professional notary visit. Notary visits are typically conducted in the attorney/professional visitation area, separate from regular family visitation. If both a family member and the inmate must sign the same document, call us to discuss whether the facility allows an alternate arrangement.

Do you do weekend or evening notary visits at jails?

Weekend and evening availability depends entirely on the facility's professional visitation hours. Some county jails accommodate weekend professional visits with advance notice; others restrict access to weekdays. We confirm the facility's schedule and available appointment windows when you call.

What does a jail notary visit cost?

LA County jails start at $200–$250 per appointment depending on location and number of documents. Out-of-county facilities are quoted individually based on travel distance and coordination requirements. Federal facilities may carry a higher fee due to additional coordination. Pricing includes travel, wait time, clearance coordination, and notarization — call or text for a specific quote.

Is mail-in notarization available for inmates?

Yes, for eligible documents. Our mail-in process at apostille.mobileamericannotary.com allows documents to be mailed to the facility, signed in the presence of authorized facility staff, and returned to us for completion. Not all documents or facilities support mail-in execution — call us to confirm whether your document and facility qualify.

What happens if the inmate is transferred before the appointment?

If an inmate is transferred between booking and the scheduled appointment, you may still be responsible for a trip fee if we are already en route. To reduce this risk, we recommend confirming the inmate's location with the facility on the day of the appointment, especially when transfer is possible. We will rebook at the new facility as quickly as clearance allows.

Do you serve jails outside Los Angeles County?

Yes. We serve facilities across Orange, Riverside, San Bernardino, Santa Barbara, and Ventura Counties in addition to all six major LA County jails. Extended-area facilities may carry additional travel fees. Call or text (213) 933-2507 for a quote specific to your facility.
