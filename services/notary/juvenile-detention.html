How access works

Juvenile facilities require attorney coordination — here’s how we handle it.

Adult jails allow outside notaries to enter as professional visitors on their own. Juvenile facilities in California are more restricted. The youth’s attorney of record must be involved for an outside notary to enter and meet with the youth. This protects the youth and complies with Welfare & Institutions Code § 827 confidentiality rules. There are two paths, and both start with a phone call from you:

A

Court order via the attorney

The youth’s defense attorney files a short request with the juvenile court for an order authorizing a specifically-named notary to enter the facility for a professional visit. Once signed by the judge, we schedule the visit directly with the facility. This is the standard path when the attorney will not be present at the signing.

B

Attorney sponsors the visit

The attorney schedules their own professional visit with the youth and brings the notary in as part of that visit. The notary enters under the attorney’s credentials, meets the youth in the attorney/professional visiting area, and completes the notarization while the attorney is present.

—

You call us first

Either way, the family drives the call. Tell us the youth’s name, the facility, and what needs to be signed. Give us your attorney’s name and phone number and we’ll reach out to them directly, or have your attorney call us. We handle the coordination so you don’t have to.

Juvenile Hall vs. SYTF

Two facility types — two very different situations.

Since California closed the Division of Juvenile Justice (DJJ) in 2023 and counties took over long-term custody, juvenile facilities in this state fall into two categories. Understanding which one your youth is in helps you know what documents are typically needed and how urgent the timeline is.

1

Juvenile Hall (pre-adjudication)

Your youth has been detained and the case has not yet been decided by the court. Stays are usually shorter (days to weeks). Documents tend to be urgent: emergency Powers of Attorney so a parent can access the youth’s bank account, school, or medical providers; custodial affidavits authorizing a specific adult to make decisions; affidavits attached to a defense motion. In LA County the primary Juvenile Hall is Los Padrinos in Downey.

2

Secure Youth Treatment Facility (SYTF, post-adjudication)

The court has committed the youth to a long-term stay — usually many months, sometimes running until age 25 under the SYTF framework that replaced the old DJJ. Documents look more like adult jail work: vehicle titles when a car needs to be sold or transferred, real estate documents, family law (including custody of the youth’s own children), education and college paperwork for youth turning 18 inside, and long-term financial Powers of Attorney. LA County SYTFs are Barry J. Nidorf, Campus Kilpatrick, and Dorothy Kirby.

3

Combined facilities (OC, San Bernardino, Riverside)

Outside LA County, the other Southern California counties we serve typically operate a single facility (or a facility with an adjacent treatment center) that houses both pre-adjudication and committed youth. For those pages we cover both situations. See Orange County Juvenile Hall, San Bernardino Central Valley JDAC, and Riverside Juvenile Hall.

Confidentiality & WIC 827

Juvenile records are sealed — here’s what that means for our visits.

California Welfare & Institutions Code § 827 keeps juvenile case files confidential. Only specific people (parents, guardians, the youth’s attorney, certain officials) can see the record. That confidentiality is one of the reasons juvenile facilities are more restricted than adult jails and why an attorney is involved in every visit.

✓

We do not access sealed records

We do not request, review, or handle any juvenile court records. Our role is limited to identifying the signer and completing the notarization of a document that you or your attorney provides to us.

✓

Our notary journal is neutral

California notary law requires us to log every notarization in an official journal. That entry records the type of document, the signer, and the date — not the contents of the document or anything about the case. Journal entries for juvenile visits look the same as any other notarization.

✓

Facility privacy protocols respected

Every facility has rules about what visitors can bring in, what can be said in visitation areas, and how to move through secured spaces. We follow every posted rule and every instruction from facility staff.

Mobile Notary FAQ — Juvenile Detention

Frequently asked questions.

How does a mobile notary get into a juvenile detention facility?

Juvenile facilities in California are more restricted than adult jails and generally require an attorney's involvement. Two paths: (1) the youth’s defense attorney requests a court order authorizing the notary to enter, or (2) the attorney brings the notary in as part of their own professional visit. Either way, the family calls us first — give us your attorney’s contact info, or have your attorney reach out to us, and we handle the coordination.

Can a minor sign a notarized document while detained?

In many cases yes — but it depends on the document, the age of the youth, and whether a parent or guardian needs to co-sign. California notary law does not automatically prevent a minor from signing; a notary can notarize the signature of a competent signer regardless of age. The document’s legal effect is a separate question best confirmed with your attorney. We frequently notarize signatures of youth over 18 who are still in a Secure Youth Treatment Facility, and parental-consent forms signed by parents on behalf of younger children.

What is the difference between a Juvenile Hall and a Secure Youth Treatment Facility (SYTF)?

A Juvenile Hall holds youth before their case has been decided (pre-adjudication) — the equivalent of pre-trial detention. Stays are usually shorter and documents tend to be urgent. A Secure Youth Treatment Facility (SYTF) holds youth after the court has committed them (post-adjudication) — longer stays, sometimes running until age 25 under the framework that replaced the old Division of Juvenile Justice. In LA County since 2023, Los Padrinos is the primary Juvenile Hall and Barry J. Nidorf, Campus Kilpatrick, and Dorothy Kirby are SYTFs. Orange, San Bernardino, and Riverside Counties operate facilities that hold both.

Do you access juvenile court records or sealed files?

No. Juvenile records in California are protected by Welfare & Institutions Code § 827 and are confidential — we do not request, view, or handle those records. We notarize signatures on documents you or your attorney provides. What’s inside those documents is between your family and your attorney; our role is limited to identifying the signer and completing the notarization.

Same-day service is possible in urgent situations, but juvenile facility access requires attorney coordination first — which usually takes at least a day. Call us as early as you can. We start the attorney conversation immediately and confirm the earliest available window.

Are you affiliated with LA County Probation or any juvenile facility?

No. We are an independent California-commissioned mobile notary service. We are not part of any county probation department or juvenile facility. We work through each facility’s established professional-visitor procedures, coordinated with the youth’s defense attorney.
