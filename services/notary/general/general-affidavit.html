Understanding the document

What a general affidavit does.

A general affidavit is a written statement of facts that you swear or affirm are true in front of a notary public. It is used when there is no preprinted form, but a court, agency, or organization needs your statement under oath rather than a regular letter.

Common general affidavit uses

  • Lost-document affidavits: sworn statements that an original document has been lost, destroyed, or was never received.
  • Identity affidavits: sworn statements confirming your identity or explaining name variations across different records.
  • Residency affidavits: sworn statements of where you live and how long you have lived there, used for school enrollment, benefits, or government applications.
  • Declarations in lieu of: sworn substitutes for certificates, records, or documents that are unavailable or unobtainable.
  • Employment-related sworn statements: affidavits for licensing boards, employers, or background-check purposes.

Why an affidavit is requested instead of a letter

  • The requesting party needs your statement under oath, not just a casual written explanation.
  • There is no official preprinted form for your specific situation, but sworn facts are still required.
  • A court, judge, agency, or attorney has asked you to submit a sworn written statement.
  • The affidavit provides stronger assurance of accuracy because it is signed under oath and notarized.
  • The document may later be submitted to a foreign authority and require an Apostille.

Notarization details

How general affidavits and sworn statements are notarized.

General affidavits almost always require a jurat — the notarial act where you swear or affirm the contents are true and sign in the notary's presence. The notary witnesses your oath and signature but does not verify the truthfulness of the facts you state.

Before the signing

  • The affidavit should be completed before signing, but left unsigned until the notary is present.
  • The signer must present valid, current government-issued photo identification.
  • Any case numbers, claim numbers, or reference numbers connected to the statement should be included in the document.
  • If the requesting party has specific wording requirements, those instructions should be available at the appointment.

During the notarization appointment

  1. The notary confirms the signer's identity using current government-issued photo ID.
  2. The signer confirms the affidavit is complete and accurate before signing.
  3. The notary administers an oath or affirmation, as required by a jurat.
  4. The signer signs the affidavit in the notary's presence.
  5. The notary completes the jurat certificate, applies the seal, and records the act in the journal.

Important: A notary witnesses your oath and signature — they do not decide whether your affidavit answers the legal questions in your case, verify the facts you are swearing to, or advise on what to include. If you need help with wording or legal strategy, speak with an attorney or the person who requested the affidavit.

FAQ

General affidavit notary FAQ

What is a general affidavit or sworn statement?

A general affidavit or sworn statement is a written declaration of facts that you swear or affirm are true in front of a notary public. It is used when an organization, court, or agency needs your statement under oath but does not provide a specific preprinted form.

What type of notarization does a general affidavit require?

Most general affidavits require a jurat, because you are swearing that the contents are true. The notary administers an oath or affirmation, witnesses the signature, completes the jurat certificate, and applies their seal.

Does the notary verify that what I say in the affidavit is true?

No. The notary witnesses your oath and signature — they do not investigate, verify, or confirm the truthfulness of the facts you are swearing to. Their role is to identify you, administer the oath, watch you sign, and complete the notarial certificate.

What can a general affidavit be used for?

General affidavits can be used for lost-document affidavits, identity affidavits, residency affidavits, declarations in lieu of, employment sworn statements, court filings, insurance claims, and supporting immigration or family law matters. The requesting party will usually tell you what the affidavit needs to address.

Can the affidavit be drafted and notarized at the same appointment?

Yes. A mobile office setup with a laptop, portable printer, and scanner can be brought to the appointment so you can access a template from your attorney or an online legal-document service, complete it, print it, sign it, and have it notarized in one visit. The notary does not draft the document or provide legal advice.

Can a notarized general affidavit be apostilled for use in another country?

Yes. A notarized general affidavit can often be apostilled or legalized for use at a foreign consulate, court, or government office. This is common when a foreign authority requires a sworn statement from you. We can coordinate the Apostille after notarization.

Can I get a general affidavit notarized the same day?

Often yes, depending on the location, timing, and whether the document is already prepared. Calling or texting usually gets the fastest scheduling response.

What ID does the signer need to bring?

The signer must present valid, current government-issued photo identification. A California driver's license, state ID card, or unexpired U.S. passport are the most common acceptable forms. The name on the ID must match the name on the affidavit.
