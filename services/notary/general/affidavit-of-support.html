Understanding the document

What an immigration affidavit of support does.

An immigration affidavit of support is a sworn statement in which a sponsor promises to provide financial support for an immigrant so they will not become a public charge. It is used to demonstrate to the government that the person being sponsored has financial backing.

Common affidavit of support uses

  • Family-based green card sponsorships: sponsor affidavits for spouses, children, parents, and other relatives.
  • Fiancé visas: financial support documentation for K-1 and K-3 visa cases.
  • Consular processing: sworn support statements for cases handled at a U.S. embassy or consulate overseas.
  • Adjustment of status: supporting affidavits filed alongside applications processed inside the United States.
  • Separate sponsor letters: personal letters of financial support that must be sworn and notarized.

Common forms and document types

  • Form I-864: official USCIS Affidavit of Support for most family-based green card cases. Signed under penalty of perjury — typically does not require notarization.
  • Form I-134: Affidavit of Support often used for certain temporary visa categories.
  • Attorney-drafted affidavits: sworn statements tailored to a specific case, often requiring notarization.
  • Personal support letters: letters explaining the sponsor's relationship and financial commitment, which may require notarization when submitted to a consulate or agency.

Notarization requirements

When immigration affidavits of support require notarization.

Not every immigration document needs a notary. The official I-864 does not. But separate sworn support letters and financial affidavits often do, especially when a consulate or immigration attorney requests them.

Documents often requiring notarization

  • Separate affidavits that explain the sponsor's financial ability to support the immigrant.
  • Letters of support from friends or family members that must serve as sworn statements.
  • Affidavits of residence, relationship, or employment submitted alongside an immigration application.
  • Financial affidavits required by a foreign consulate or government office.
  • Support letters that will be apostilled for use in a Hague Convention country.

During the notarization appointment

  1. The notary confirms the sponsor's identity using current government-issued photo ID.
  2. The sponsor confirms the document is complete and accurate before signing.
  3. The notary administers an oath or affirmation, as required by a jurat.
  4. The sponsor signs the affidavit in the notary's presence.
  5. The notary completes the notarial certificate, applies the seal, and records the act in the journal.

Important: A notary can identify the certificate type and complete the notarial act, but cannot review your immigration eligibility, advise on what to include in the affidavit, or tell the sponsor which documents are required for their specific case. Always confirm notarization requirements with your attorney or the requesting agency.

FAQ

Affidavit of support notary FAQ

Does Form I-864 need to be notarized?

Generally no. Form I-864 is signed under penalty of perjury and does not require notarization in most family-based green card cases. However, separate support letters, personal financial affidavits, or other sworn statements that accompany the file often do require notarization, especially when a consulate or attorney requests it.

What type of notarization does an affidavit of support use?

Most immigration affidavits and support letters that require notarization use a jurat, because the sponsor is swearing the information is true. The notary administers an oath or affirmation, witnesses the signature, completes the jurat certificate, and applies their seal.

What ID does the sponsor need to bring?

The sponsor must present valid, current government-issued photo identification. A California driver's license, state ID card, or unexpired U.S. passport are the most common acceptable forms. The name on the ID must match the name on the affidavit.

Can I complete and notarize the affidavit at the same appointment?

Yes. A mobile office setup with a laptop, portable printer, and scanner can be brought to the appointment so the sponsor can access a template from their attorney or an online legal-document service, complete it, print it, sign it, and have it notarized in one visit. The notary does not draft the document or provide legal advice.

Can a notarized affidavit of support be apostilled for consular use?

Yes. A notarized immigration affidavit or support letter can often be apostilled or legalized for use at a foreign consulate or government office. This is common when a foreign authority requires a sworn, notarized, and apostilled financial statement from a U.S.-based sponsor.

Can I get an affidavit of support notarized the same day?

Often yes, depending on the location, timing, and whether the document is already prepared. Calling or texting usually gets the fastest scheduling response.
