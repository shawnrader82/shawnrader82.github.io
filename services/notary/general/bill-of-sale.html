Understanding the document

What a bill of sale does.

A bill of sale records the transfer of ownership from one party to another and creates a written record of what was sold, when it was sold, and what terms were agreed.

Common bill of sale uses

  • Vehicle sales: cars, trucks, motorcycles, and other private-party transfers.
  • Boat and vessel sales: boats, trailers, and watercraft transactions.
  • Equipment and machinery: heavy equipment, tools, and business assets.
  • Personal property: valuable items sold directly between private parties.
  • Supporting transfer records: added documentation when title or ownership proof matters.

What the document should include

  • Buyer and seller names and contact information.
  • Description of the item being sold, including year, make, and model when relevant.
  • VIN, HIN, serial number, or other identifying number whenever applicable.
  • Sale date, price, payment terms, and any “as-is” or warranty language the parties choose.
  • Signature blocks and any attached notarial certificate wording if notarization is being requested.

California requirements

What to expect when notarizing a bill of sale.

California DMV materials recognize bill of sale use in vehicle and vessel transactions, and forms such as REG 135 and REG 262 often come up, but a standard bill of sale is generally not required to be notarized or witnessed.

Before the signing

  • The document should be completed before signing, but left unsigned until the notary is present.
  • The signer must present valid, current government-issued photo identification.
  • Any title, lien, registration, or transfer paperwork tied to the sale should be available.
  • If the title is missing, related DMV transfer forms may also come into play.

During notarization

  1. The notary confirms the signer’s identity and willingness to sign.
  2. The notary reviews the certificate wording already attached to the document.
  3. The signer signs in the notary’s presence.
  4. The notary completes the certificate, applies the seal, and records the act in the journal.
  5. The completed document is returned immediately for the sale or transfer process.

Important: A notary can identify the notarial certificate and complete the notarial act, but cannot choose the legal form, draft custom sale terms, advise on tax or title strategy, or tell either party which transaction language to use.

FAQ

Bill of sale notary FAQ

Does a bill of sale need to be notarized in California?

Usually not for a standard California transfer. California DMV materials allow a bill of sale without notarization or witnessing in the ordinary transfer process, though some parties still choose notarization for stronger proof.

Which California DMV forms commonly come up?

REG 135 is a California bill of sale form, REG 262 includes a bill of sale section, and REG 227 may also matter when the original title is missing.

What type of notarization is usually used for a bill of sale?

Many bill of sale documents that include notarial wording use an acknowledgment, but the notary follows the certificate wording attached to the document presented at the appointment.

Can you meet where the transaction is happening?

Yes. Many bill of sale appointments happen at homes, offices, parking lots, marinas, storage facilities, mechanic shops, or other agreed meeting locations.

Can you bring a laptop and printer if the document is not ready yet?

Yes. A mobile office setup can be brought so the parties can access, print, sign, and notarize the document in one visit. The service is for convenience and logistics, not legal document selection or advice.

Can you help with same-day bill of sale appointments?

Often yes, depending on the location, timing, and the number of signers involved. Calling or texting usually gets the fastest scheduling response.
