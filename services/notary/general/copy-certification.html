Understanding the document

What copy certification by document custodian does.

Copy certification by document custodian is the process where the person holding the original document signs a sworn statement that a photocopy is a true, correct, and complete copy of the original — and the notary notarizes that sworn statement using a jurat. The notary does not certify the copy itself.

Common documents people request copy certification for

  • Passports: for employers, banks, schools, and foreign authorities requiring proof of identity.
  • School transcripts and diplomas: when the issuing institution cannot provide a new certified copy in time.
  • Professional licenses and certifications: for employment verification, foreign registration, or board applications.
  • Immigration documents: residency cards, visas, and other personal immigration records.
  • Business records: contracts, corporate resolutions, invoices, and other business documents.
  • Driver licenses and state ID cards: for financial institutions or other parties requiring identity confirmation.

How copy certification by custodian differs from a certified copy

  • With a traditional certified copy, the notary personally certifies that the copy matches the original — California law does not allow this for most documents.
  • With copy certification by document custodian, the document holder is the one swearing the copy is accurate.
  • The notary's role is to witness the sworn statement, administer the oath, and complete the jurat certificate — not to certify the underlying document.
  • Many banks, employers, schools, and foreign authorities accept this method, but the requesting party should always be confirmed beforehand.

California requirements and limits

What California notaries can and cannot certify.

California law limits which documents a notary can directly certify a copy of. For most documents, direct copy certification is not permitted, making the document custodian method the standard approach. Certain documents are excluded entirely and must come from the issuing agency.

Documents excluded from copy certification by custodian

  • Vital records: birth certificates, marriage certificates, and death certificates must be obtained directly from the county recorder, state, or vital records agency — a custodian sworn statement is not a substitute.
  • Court records: court-certified copies must come from the court itself. A notarized custodian statement will not be accepted as a substitute for a court-certified document.
  • Other official public records: records that must come from the agency that issued them are similarly excluded.
  • Always confirm with the requesting party whether the custodian method will be accepted before scheduling.

During the copy certification appointment

  1. The document custodian presents the original and the copy (or the copy is made on-site).
  2. The custodian carefully compares the copy to the original and confirms it is true, correct, and complete.
  3. The notary confirms the custodian's identity using current government-issued photo ID.
  4. The custodian signs the sworn statement in the notary's presence.
  5. The notary administers an oath or affirmation, completes the jurat certificate, applies the seal, and records the act in the journal.

Important: A notary can complete the jurat notarization on the custodian's sworn statement, but cannot advise on whether the requesting party will accept the result, guarantee that the copy will be honored, or certify vital records or court documents under any circumstances.

FAQ

Copy certification by document custodian FAQ

What is copy certification by document custodian?

It is a process where the holder of the original document signs a sworn statement that a photocopy is a true, correct, and complete copy of the original. The notary does not certify the copy directly — the notary notarizes the custodian's sworn statement using a jurat.

Why is this method used in California instead of direct copy certification?

California law heavily limits when notaries can directly certify copies. For most documents, the notary may not certify the copy. Copy certification by document custodian provides a lawful alternative that keeps the notary within state law while still giving the requesting party a sworn, notarized statement about the copy.

Can a California notary certify copies of vital records or court records?

No. California notaries cannot certify copies of vital records — birth, marriage, or death certificates — or copies of court records. Those must come directly from the issuing agency. Copy certification by document custodian is also not a substitute for those official records.

What documents are commonly handled with copy certification by custodian?

Common examples include passports, driver licenses, state ID cards, diplomas, school transcripts, professional licenses, immigration documents, and business records — as long as the requesting party is willing to accept a custodian certification.

What type of notarization is used for this process?

Copy certification by document custodian uses a jurat. The custodian swears the copy is true, correct, and complete, the notary administers an oath or affirmation, witnesses the signature, completes the jurat certificate, and applies the seal.

Can I get a copy certification appointment the same day?

Often yes, depending on the location and timing. Same-day and next-day appointments are frequently available. Calling or texting usually gets the fastest scheduling response.
