Understanding the document

What a name change affidavit does.

A name change affidavit — also called a same-name affidavit — is a sworn statement in which the signer declares under oath that two or more names refer to the same person. The affidavit does not change a legal name by itself; it explains why different versions exist and connects them to a single identity.

Common reasons names differ across documents

  • Marriage: last name changed after marriage, creating a mismatch between older records and current ID.
  • Divorce: maiden name restored, but prior records still reflect the married name.
  • Court order: legal name change not yet reflected in all documents.
  • Naturalization: name adjusted or anglicized during the citizenship process.
  • Spelling variations: transliteration differences, accents, or clerical inconsistencies across documents issued in different countries.

Where these affidavits are commonly used

  • Passport applications: passport name mismatches with marriage certificate or birth certificate.
  • School transcripts: diploma or transcript issued under a former name for employment or licensing verification.
  • Banking discrepancies: investment, retirement, or bank account records with inconsistent name history.
  • Immigration paperwork: USCIS or NVC file shows different name versions; foreign consulate requests name reconciliation.
  • SSA records: Social Security Administration records that have not yet been updated after a legal change.

Notarization details

How a name change affidavit is notarized.

The notary witnesses the signer's oath and signature — not the truth of the underlying claim. Notarization confirms that the person who appeared, presented acceptable ID, and swore to the statement is the same person who signed the document.

Before the signing

  • The affidavit should be completed but left unsigned until the notary is present.
  • The signer must present valid, current government-issued photo ID matching at least one of the names in the affidavit.
  • If both old and new name IDs are available, bringing both is helpful but not required.
  • Any attorney instructions, consulate requirements, or institution-specific wording should be incorporated in the document before the appointment.

During notarization

  1. The notary confirms the signer's identity using the government-issued photo ID.
  2. The signer confirms the affidavit is complete and accurate before signing.
  3. The notary administers an oath or affirmation, as required for a jurat.
  4. The signer signs the affidavit in the notary's presence.
  5. The notary completes the notarial certificate, applies the seal, and records the act in the journal.

Important: A notary can identify the certificate type and complete the notarial act, but cannot advise on whether the affidavit language is sufficient for a specific agency, court, consulate, or institution. Confirm wording requirements with your attorney or the requesting party before the appointment.

FAQ

Name change affidavit notary FAQ

What is a name change affidavit or same-name affidavit?

A name change affidavit or same-name affidavit is a sworn statement confirming that two or more different name versions all refer to the same person. It is used when a current legal name does not exactly match the name on a passport, transcript, bank account, or other official document.

When do I need a name change or same-name affidavit?

Common situations include passport applications with a name mismatch against a marriage certificate, school transcripts issued under a former name, banking or retirement account discrepancies, immigration paperwork where USCIS or a consulate flags name differences, and SSA records not yet updated after a legal name change.

Does a name change affidavit need to be notarized?

Most name change and same-name affidavits are sworn statements and are expected to be notarized. Notarization adds credibility and helps agencies, banks, consulates, and institutions accept that the sworn statement is genuine.

What type of notarization does a name change affidavit require?

Most name change affidavits use a jurat, because the signer is swearing or affirming that the information is true. The notary administers an oath or affirmation and witnesses the signing. If the form calls for an acknowledgment instead, the notary follows that wording.

What ID is needed at the appointment?

The signer must present valid, current government-issued photo identification matching at least one of the names referenced in the affidavit. A California driver's license, state ID, or unexpired U.S. passport are the most common acceptable forms.

Can a name change affidavit be apostilled for use in another country?

Yes. After the name change or same-name affidavit is notarized, it can often be Apostilled or legalized for use overseas. This is common when a foreign consulate, embassy, or bank needs proof that documents issued under different names belong to the same person.
