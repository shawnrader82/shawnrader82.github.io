Understanding the document

What a power of attorney does.

A power of attorney authorizes one person to act on behalf of another person for specific legal, financial, property, or healthcare matters, depending on the wording of the document.

Common POA types

  • General POA: broad authority over financial or legal matters, usually ending if the principal becomes incapacitated.
  • Durable POA: remains effective even if the principal later becomes incapacitated. See our estate planning Power of Attorney page for durable, springing, and limited POA details.
  • Limited POA: authority is limited to a specific task, property, or timeframe.
  • Financial POA: often used for banking, bill payment, investments, or property management.
  • Medical or healthcare POA: authorizes healthcare decision-making when the principal cannot communicate.

When people commonly need one

  • An elderly parent needs help managing banking, bills, or property matters.
  • A hospitalized or recovering signer cannot easily leave their location.
  • A family wants to complete long-term planning before surgery, travel, or care placement.
  • A limited POA is needed for a specific transaction, vehicle matter, or real estate-related task.
  • A client needs the document signed urgently and wants everything handled in one visit.

What to expect when notarizing a power of attorney.

The exact certificate wording on the document controls the notarial act, and POA documents often require more care because institutions tend to review them closely.

Before the signing

  • The document should be completed before signing, but left unsigned until the notary is present.
  • The signer must present valid, current government-issued photo identification.
  • If the document calls for witnesses, they should be arranged ahead of time and be present when needed.
  • If the signer is in a hospital or care setting, mention any timing, visitor, or mobility restrictions when booking.

During notarization

  1. The notary confirms the signer’s identity and willingness to sign.
  2. The notary reviews the certificate wording already attached to the document.
  3. The signer signs in the notary’s presence.
  4. The notary completes the certificate, applies the seal, and records the act in the journal.
  5. For POA documents, the required journal entry details are handled at the appointment.

Important: A notary can explain the notarial certificate and procedural steps, but cannot choose the form, interpret legal language, or advise which powers should be granted. Those decisions belong to the client, attorney, or requesting institution.

FAQ

Power of attorney notary FAQ

Does a power of attorney need to be notarized in California?

A power of attorney is commonly notarized because most banks, financial institutions, and agencies prefer or require it that way in practice. If the document involves real estate or the receiving institution specifically requires notarization, a notarized POA is usually the safest path.

What type of notarization is usually used for a POA?

Many power of attorney documents use an acknowledgment, but the notary follows the certificate wording attached to the document. If the wording raises a procedural issue, that can be identified during the appointment.

Can you come to a hospital, rehab, or assisted living facility?

Yes. Many POA signings happen in medical or care settings. Let us know the facility, room access details, and whether the signer is able to present ID and communicate clearly.

Can you bring a laptop and printer if the POA is not prepared yet?

Yes. A mobile office setup can be brought so the client can access, print, sign, and notarize the document in one visit. The service is for convenience and logistics, not legal document selection or advice.

Can I get certified copies of my power of attorney?

Yes. If you expect to provide copies to more than one bank, agency, or institution, mention that before the appointment so the visit can be planned accordingly.

Can you help if the document also needs county clerk handling?

Yes. If county clerk filing, document delivery, recording support, or related follow-up is part of the job, mention that before booking so the scope and timing can be reviewed.
