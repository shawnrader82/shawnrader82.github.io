Understanding the document

What a guardianship or temporary custody authorization does.

A guardianship or temporary custody authorization is a written document signed by a parent or legal guardian that grants a trusted adult temporary authority to care for a minor child and make routine decisions on that child's behalf. It is not a court order and does not transfer or permanently change parental rights.

Common document types

  • California Caregiver's Authorization Affidavit (Family Code §6550): allows a relative or non-relative caregiver to enroll a child in school and consent to school-related medical care.
  • Temporary custody authorization letter: written permission from a parent delegating day-to-day care authority to a grandparent, aunt, uncle, or other trusted adult.
  • School enrollment authorization: permits a caregiver to enroll and manage a child's school attendance while the parent is away.
  • Medical authorization for a minor: allows a caregiver to consent to routine medical, dental, or emergency treatment on behalf of the child.
  • Military deployment care letter: used by a deploying parent to formalize childcare arrangements during service-related absence.
  • Summer-with-relatives letter: authorizes a grandparent or other relative to care for a child during a vacation period.

Important distinction

  • These documents represent a parent's voluntary written permission — they are not court orders.
  • They grant a trusted adult temporary, limited authority over routine care decisions for a minor.
  • They do not terminate or transfer parental rights and are generally revocable by the parent.
  • Court-ordered legal guardianship is an entirely different proceeding handled through the probate court with the assistance of an attorney. If you need permanent guardianship, consult a family law or probate attorney.
  • The scope of authority (school, medical, or both) depends on how the document is written and which statute applies.

Use cases

When parents and guardians need this type of document notarized.

Many situations call for a notarized authorization rather than an unnotarized one, especially when a hospital, government agency, or out-of-state institution is involved.

1

Medical treatment and hospital visits

Hospitals and urgent care facilities frequently request or require a notarized medical authorization before honoring a caregiver's consent for a minor patient. A notarized document speeds up the process and reduces the chance of a delay in care.

2

School enrollment and participation

A California Caregiver's Authorization Affidavit (Family Code §6550) allows a relative or non-relative caregiver to enroll a child in school. While the school may accept an unnotarized affidavit, many districts and school administrators prefer or request a notarized version for their records.

3

Military deployment and extended travel

A deploying parent or a parent traveling for an extended period often needs a notarized care authorization so that a grandparent, relative, or designated caregiver can manage the child's school, medical, and daily needs with confidence.

4

Relative caregiving arrangements

Grandparents, aunts, uncles, and other relatives caring for a child while the parents are temporarily unavailable often carry a notarized authorization letter to demonstrate their authority when dealing with schools, clinics, and other institutions.

California requirements

What to expect when notarizing a guardianship or caregiver authorization.

California notary law requires that the signer appear in person, present acceptable identification, and sign willingly and knowingly. The notary verifies identity and witnesses the signature — the notary does not draft the document or advise on its legal content.

Before the signing

  • The document should be fully completed before signing, but left unsigned until the notary is present.
  • Each signer must have a valid, current government-issued photo ID available.
  • If both parents are expected to sign, confirm their availability and location before scheduling.
  • The document should already include notarial certificate wording (acknowledgment or jurat) — the notary cannot add certificate language to a document that has none.

During notarization

  1. The notary confirms each signer's identity using the government-issued photo ID.
  2. The notary verifies that the signer is signing voluntarily and understands the document.
  3. The signer signs in the notary's presence.
  4. The notary completes the notarial certificate, applies the seal, and records the act in the journal.
  5. The completed document is returned immediately to the signer for delivery to the school, hospital, or caregiver.

Important: A notary can complete the notarial act but cannot draft the authorization document, advise on what authority it grants, or tell you which form to use for your specific custody or caregiving situation. Obtain the document from your attorney, the receiving institution, or an online legal-document service before the appointment.

FAQ

Guardianship & temporary custody notary FAQ

Is a guardianship or caregiver authorization the same as court-ordered legal guardianship?

No. A notarized caregiver authorization or temporary custody letter is a parent's or guardian's written permission that grants a trusted adult temporary authority to care for a child. It is not a court order and does not transfer or terminate parental rights. Court-ordered legal guardianship is a separate legal proceeding that goes through the probate court with an attorney. If permanent or court-supervised guardianship is needed, consult a family law or probate attorney.

Does a California Caregiver's Authorization Affidavit (Family Code §6550) need to be notarized?

California Family Code §6550 does not require notarization for the affidavit to be legally effective. Schools generally accept an unnotarized affidavit for enrollment. However, hospitals and medical providers frequently request or require notarization before honoring a caregiver's medical authorization for a minor. Non-relative caregivers should also be aware that Part 2 of the affidavit limits medical authority compared to Part 1 — an attorney can clarify the scope for your specific situation.

How long is a temporary custody or caregiver authorization valid?

The duration depends on how the document is written. Many authorizations remain valid until revoked in writing by the parent. Others are written with an explicit end date tied to a school year, trip, or deployment period. Schools and agencies may ask for a new or updated authorization annually. Check with the receiving institution before the appointment if you are unsure how long the document needs to remain current.

Do both parents need to sign a temporary custody or caregiver authorization?

Best practice is for both parents to sign whenever both hold custodial rights, since a school, hospital, or agency may question an authorization signed by only one parent. The requirement depends on the custody arrangement, the type of authorization, and what the receiving institution expects. Check with the school, hospital, or your attorney before the appointment to confirm what signatures are required.

What ID does the signing parent or guardian need?

Each signer must present a valid, current government-issued photo ID. Acceptable forms include a California driver's license, California ID card, U.S. passport or passport card, military ID, or another qualifying government-issued photo ID that satisfies California notary law requirements. The ID must not be expired at the time of signing.

Can you bring a notary to the hospital, my home, or the caregiver's home?

Yes. As a mobile notary service, appointments can be scheduled at homes, hospitals, care facilities, schools, offices, and other agreed locations throughout Los Angeles County. Call or text with the location and the document type to arrange the visit.

Can a notary draft this document for me?

No. A California notary public cannot draft legal documents, provide legal advice, or choose a form on your behalf. Obtain the authorization template from your attorney, from the receiving school or hospital, from a California court self-help center, or from an online legal-document service. Once you have a completed, unsigned document, the notary can verify identity, witness the signature, and complete the notarial certificate.
