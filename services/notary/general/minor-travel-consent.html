Understanding the document

What a minor travel consent letter does.

A minor travel consent letter — also called a child travel consent letter — is a written authorization from one or both parents or legal guardians permitting a minor to travel without them. It serves as proof for border agents, airlines, and foreign immigration officials that the child has not been taken without parental permission.

Common use cases

  • International travel with one parent: U.S. CBP recommends a notarized consent letter from the non-traveling parent when a child crosses the U.S. border with only one parent.
  • Travel with grandparents or relatives: consent letter showing the accompanying adult has parental authorization.
  • Divorced or separated parent situations: written authorization from the parent not present for the trip.
  • School trips and organized group travel: some schools and youth organizations require a signed and notarized consent form.
  • Travel without either parent: both parents or legal guardians typically need to provide consent when the child is traveling with another adult entirely.

What the letter should include

  • Child's full legal name and date of birth.
  • Passport number for international travel.
  • Travel dates, departure and return.
  • Destination countries or regions covered by the authorization.
  • Full name and relationship of the accompanying adult.
  • Contact information for the consenting parent or guardian.
  • A clear consent statement — such as "I authorize my child to travel with…"
  • Parent or guardian's signature signed in the notary's presence.

What officials typically expect

What border agents and foreign immigration ask for.

U.S. Customs and Border Protection and foreign immigration authorities have standard expectations when a minor is crossing an international border without one or both parents. A notarized letter that covers these elements is far less likely to cause delays at checkpoints.

What CBP and foreign immigration typically look for

  • A notarized letter — not just a typed or handwritten note — confirming parental authorization.
  • Both parents' signatures when the child is traveling without either parent.
  • The non-traveling parent's notarized consent when the child travels with only one parent.
  • Specific travel dates and named destinations, not open-ended authorization.
  • Contact information for the consenting parent so officials can follow up if needed.
  • A copy of the parent's or guardian's ID attached to support identity of the signer.

During the notarization appointment

  1. The consenting parent or guardian presents valid, current government-issued photo ID.
  2. The notary confirms the signer's identity and willingness to sign.
  3. The parent or guardian signs the letter in the notary's presence — do not sign beforehand.
  4. The notary completes the notarial certificate (typically an acknowledgment or jurat depending on the document language), applies the seal, and records the act in the journal.
  5. The completed, notarized original is returned immediately for use at the border or with the airline.

Important: Only the consenting parent or guardian needs to appear at the notary appointment. The child and the accompanying adult do not need to be present. A notary can complete the notarial act but cannot advise on what specific language a particular country requires — confirm destination requirements with the relevant embassy or consulate before the appointment.

FAQ

Minor travel consent notary FAQ

Does a child travel consent letter need to be notarized?

Notarization is not always legally required for domestic travel, but U.S. Customs and Border Protection recommends a notarized consent letter when a minor crosses the U.S. border without one or both parents. Many foreign countries require notarization, and some require an Apostille as well. A notarized letter is far less likely to cause delays at checkpoints.

Does the non-traveling parent need to sign?

When a child is traveling internationally with only one parent, border agents often expect to see consent from the non-traveling parent. When a child travels without either parent, consent from both parents or legal guardians is typically expected. The letter should be signed by whoever holds legal authority to consent.

What ID does the signing parent or guardian need?

The consenting parent or guardian must present valid, current government-issued photo identification. A California driver's license, state ID card, or unexpired U.S. passport are the most common acceptable forms. The name on the ID must match the name on the consent letter.

Does the letter need to include specific travel dates and destinations?

Yes. Border agents and foreign immigration officials typically expect specific travel dates and named destinations rather than an open-ended authorization. Including both departure and return dates and the countries or regions covered makes the letter more likely to be accepted without question.

Can the letter be drafted and notarized at the same appointment?

Yes. A mobile office setup with a laptop, portable printer, and scanner can be brought to the appointment so the parent can access a template, complete it with the trip details, print it, sign it, and have it notarized in one visit. The notary does not draft the document or provide legal advice.

Can I get a minor travel consent letter notarized the same day?

Often yes, depending on the location, timing, and whether the document is already prepared. Travel consent letters are frequently needed urgently before a trip. Calling or texting usually gets the fastest scheduling response.
