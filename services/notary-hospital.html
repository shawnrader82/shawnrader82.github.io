What We Notarize

Common Hospital Documents

Tap any document below to see what it is, when it's typically needed at a hospital, and the notarial act required.

Advance Healthcare Directive
Designates a medical decision-maker and documents your wishes for end-of-life care.
Tap for details ›
When needed:Often urgently needed when a patient is admitted and cannot communicate their wishes.
Notarization:California requires either notarization or two witnesses. Notarization is the preferred and most widely accepted method.
Can we create it?Yes — using our laptop, printer, and Rocket Lawyer access at the bedside.
Medical Power of Attorney
Grants someone authority to make healthcare decisions on your behalf.
Tap for details ›
When needed:Before surgery, during hospitalization, or anytime a patient wants to designate a healthcare agent.
Notarization:Must be notarized or signed before two witnesses under California law.
Can we create it?Yes — available through Rocket Lawyer on our laptop during the visit.
Financial Power of Attorney
Grants someone authority to manage finances if you are unable to do so.
Tap for details ›
When needed:When a hospitalized patient needs someone to pay bills, manage accounts, or handle financial matters.
Notarization:Required by California Probate Code. Two witnesses also needed for statutory form.
Can we create it?Yes — we can generate and print it on-site.
HIPAA Authorization
Authorizes release of medical information to designated individuals.
Tap for details ›
When needed:When family members or caregivers need access to a patient's medical records or treatment information.
Notarization:Not always legally required, but many facilities and insurers request notarized copies for additional verification.
Can we create it?Yes — template available through our on-site document tools.
Wills & Trust Documents
Estate planning documents sometimes needed urgently during hospitalization.
Tap for details ›
When needed:When a patient wants to create or update estate documents while hospitalized.
Notarization:Trusts involving real property must be notarized. Wills benefit from a notarized self-proving affidavit (requires 2 witnesses).
Can we create it?Basic templates available. For complex estates, consult an attorney first.
Insurance, Benefits & Pension Forms
Various forms requiring notarization for insurance claims, benefits, or pension processing.
Tap for details ›
When needed:Insurance claims, disability applications, pension paperwork, or benefits forms that require a notarized signature.
Notarization:Varies by form — typically an acknowledgment or jurat as specified on the document.
Can we create it?Bring the form — we notarize it. If you need help printing a downloaded form, we can do that on-site.

How It Works at the Hospital

  1. Tell us what document you need when you book, or decide when we arrive.
  2. Pull up a template on Rocket Lawyer or your preferred source on our laptop.
  3. Review, customize, and finalize your document on screen.
  4. We print it, you sign it, and we notarize it — all at the bedside.
Important: I am a commissioned notary public, not an attorney. I do not provide legal advice, recommend specific documents, or explain your legal rights. Any help I provide with Rocket Lawyer or similar services is strictly administrative — using my laptop, printer, internet connection, and platform access to help you generate, print, and organize the forms you choose so they are ready to sign and notarize. For legal questions or to decide which documents are appropriate for your situation, please consult a licensed attorney.
Before Your Visit

What to Have Ready for a Hospital Signing

Check off what you already have. We'll walk you through anything else when you call.

Your Signing Checklist

  • Valid, unexpired government-issued photo ID for each signer
  • Hospital name, patient room number, and floor/unit
  • Your unsigned documents — do not sign before we arrive (or let us know you need on-site document creation)
  • Any additional signers or witnesses present at the hospital
  • Contact info for your attorney or document preparer (if applicable)
  • Confirm the patient is alert and able to understand the signing

Good to Know

  • Do not sign any documents before the notary arrives — signatures must be made in the notary's presence.
  • The patient must be alert and aware of what they are signing. If the patient is sedated, unconscious, or unable to communicate, we cannot proceed.
  • We work at the patient's pace — there is no rush.
  • We accept cash, Zelle, Venmo, and Square (credit/debit) at the time of service.
  • We are not attorneys and cannot provide legal advice or recommend which documents to sign.

Common Questions

Hospital Notary FAQ

Answers to the questions we hear most about hospital and medical facility notary visits.

How much does a hospital notary visit cost?

Tier 1 hospitals (San Fernando Valley and Santa Clarita) start at $195 for a standard visit. Tier 2 hospitals (Simi Valley, Thousand Oaks, Hollywood, and greater LA) start at $225. Add $50 for laptop and printer access to generate a document on-site.

Can you create documents at the hospital?

Yes. We bring a laptop, portable printer, and internet connection to every hospital appointment. You can use our Rocket Lawyer access to generate powers of attorney, healthcare directives, and other forms on-site, then sign and notarize them in the same visit.

How quickly can you get to the hospital?

For urgent situations, call or text (213) 933‑2507 directly. Tier 1 hospitals are typically 10–40 minutes from our base. Tier 2 hospitals are typically 35–60+ minutes. We prioritize hospital signings and can often accommodate same-day requests.

Do you serve nursing homes and rehab centers?

Yes. We serve hospitals, skilled nursing facilities, rehabilitation centers, assisted living communities, and other medical facilities throughout Los Angeles and Ventura County. If your facility is not listed, call us and we will confirm coverage and pricing.

What ID does the patient need?

California law requires a valid, unexpired government-issued photo ID such as a driver's license, state ID, U.S. passport, or foreign passport. If the patient does not have ID available at the hospital, contact us and we can discuss options.

What if the patient is too ill to sign?

The patient must be alert, aware of what they are signing, and able to communicate. If a patient is sedated, unconscious, or unable to understand the document, we cannot legally proceed with notarization. Contact us to discuss your specific situation.
