Key rules

California TOD Deed Requirements.

New TOD Deed

Creating a new Transfer-on-Death deed for a California residential property (1–4 units). The deed names one or more beneficiaries and must be notarized and recorded before it takes effect.

  • Must be notarized — no exceptions
  • Recorded within 60 days of signing or it is void
  • Valid for 5 years from recording date
  • Grantor retains full ownership and control while alive

Revoke a TOD Deed

The grantor can cancel a previously recorded TOD deed at any time by recording a revocation. The revocation must also be notarized and recorded before the grantor's death to be effective.

  • Requires notarized revocation document
  • Must be recorded before grantor's death
  • A new TOD deed automatically supersedes the old one

Common questions

Transfer-on-Death Deed FAQ.

What is a California Transfer-on-Death deed?

A Transfer-on-Death (TOD) deed — also called a revocable TOD deed — is a California estate planning tool that lets you transfer real property directly to a named beneficiary when you die, without going through probate. You retain full ownership and control during your lifetime.

When must a TOD deed be recorded?

A California TOD deed must be recorded with the county recorder within 60 days of the date it is signed. If it is not recorded within 60 days, it is void and cannot be used.

How long is a TOD deed valid?

A California TOD deed is valid for 5 years from the date it is recorded. After 5 years, it must be re-executed and re-recorded to remain effective.

Can I revoke a TOD deed?

Yes. The grantor can revoke a TOD deed at any time while alive and competent by recording a revocation document. The property does not transfer to the beneficiary until the grantor dies and the revocation period has passed.

Does a TOD deed avoid probate?

Yes, for the property covered by the deed. The property passes directly to the beneficiary without a court probate proceeding. However, if the beneficiary predeceases the grantor and no alternate beneficiary was named, the property may still go through probate.

Are there recording fees for a TOD deed?

Yes. The same fees apply as for other deeds: first page + additional pages + SB2 + Real Estate Fraud Prevention Fee + AB 1466. Use our calculator for county-specific amounts.

Can you notarize and record the TOD deed in the same trip?

Yes. We notarize the TOD deed at your location, then take it to the recorder's office the same day — critical for meeting the 60-day recording deadline.

Should I use a TOD deed or a living trust?

That depends on your overall estate plan. A TOD deed is simpler and less expensive to create, but a living trust offers more flexibility and can cover multiple assets. Consult an estate planning attorney to decide which is right for your situation.
