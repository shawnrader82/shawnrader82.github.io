Common questions

Certified Copies FAQ.

What is a certified copy of a recorded document?

A certified copy is an official copy of a document from the county recorder's records, stamped with a certification seal confirming it is a true and accurate copy of the original on file. It is accepted by courts, title companies, lenders, and government agencies as proof of the recorded document.

Why would I need a certified copy?

Common reasons include: refinancing (lender needs proof of deed), estate administration (proving ownership after a death), title disputes, applying for a home equity loan, or replacing a lost original deed.

How much does a certified copy cost?

The county fee per page varies: LA County charges $8 per document (plus per-page fees), Orange County $5, and Ventura County $13. Our trip fee to pick it up is additional — use the calculator for a full estimate. These fees are marked 'verify at filing' for Ventura as fees can vary by document type.

How long does it take to get a certified copy?

Walk-in requests at the recorder's office are typically processed same-day. We pick up the certified copy and return it to you.

Do I need to know the document number?

Knowing the document number, recording date, or grantor/grantee names speeds up the process significantly. If you only have the property address, we can usually locate the document from the assessor's records, but it may take additional time.

Can you get certified copies for documents recorded years ago?

Yes. County recorders maintain records going back many decades. As long as the document is on file, we can request a certified copy.

What is the difference between a certified copy and a regular photocopy?

A regular photocopy has no official standing. A certified copy bears the recorder's official seal and certification statement, making it legally acceptable for use in transactions, courts, and government proceedings.

What types of documents can you get certified copies of?

We can request certified copies of grant deeds, quitclaim deeds, deeds of trust, reconveyances, liens, FBN statements, and most other documents recorded with the county clerk-recorder.
