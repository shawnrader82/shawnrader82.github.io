What we record

Grant Deed Use Cases.

Grant deeds are used in nearly every California real property transaction where the grantor is providing an implied warranty of title.

Sale or Purchase

The grant deed from a seller to a buyer is recorded to complete the transfer of ownership at closing. Escrow typically coordinates this directly — we handle the recording leg.

  • Filed with PCOR confirming purchase price
  • SB2 may be waived for primary-residence purchase
  • Recording date establishes the legal transfer date

Family Gift or Transfer

Transferring property to a child, sibling, or other family member. The deed is notarized and recorded even when no money changes hands.

  • PCOR required — mark as gift transaction
  • Prop 19 parent-child exclusion may apply (consult attorney)
  • SB2 applies unless an exemption is claimed

Transfer Into a Trust

Moving property from individual ownership into a revocable living trust. The deed names the trust as grantee and must be recorded to fund the trust for estate planning purposes.

  • Trust Transfer Deed — grant deed variant
  • Requires certification of trust for many lenders
  • PCOR required — typically marks as non-reassessable

Common questions

Grant Deed Recording FAQ.

What is a grant deed?

A grant deed is the most common deed used in California to transfer ownership of real property. Unlike a quitclaim deed, a grant deed contains an implied warranty that the grantor has not previously transferred the property and that it is free from encumbrances made by the grantor.

Does a grant deed need to be notarized before recording?

Yes. California law requires any deed affecting real property to be notarized before the county recorder will accept it. The grantor's signature must be acknowledged before a notary public.

What fees does the county charge to record a grant deed?

LA County charges $15 for the first page and $3 for each additional page, plus $75 SB2, $10 Real Estate Fraud Prevention Fee, and $2 AB 1466. Use our calculator for an exact estimate.

How long does grant deed recording take?

Walk-in same-day recording is available at most county offices. The deed is stamped and dated on the day it is presented. The original is typically mailed back to the grantee within 4–6 weeks.

What is a PCOR and do I need one?

A Preliminary Change of Ownership Report (PCOR) is a California form that must accompany most deeds when recorded. It notifies the county assessor of the transfer so they can determine whether a property tax reassessment is required.

Do you advise on property tax reassessment?

No. We are not attorneys or tax advisors. Questions about Proposition 19 exclusions, reassessment, or tax consequences belong to your attorney or CPA.

Can you notarize and record the grant deed in the same trip?

Yes. If your document still needs notarization, we notarize it first and take it to the recorder's office in the same trip — saving you time and a second appointment.

What if the recorder rejects my deed?

If the recorder rejects a document for formatting or content reasons, we notify you immediately and explain what correction is needed. County filing fees are non-refundable but we will help you understand the rejection.
