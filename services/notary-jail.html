Why jail notarizations are different

Time-Sensitive Documents That Cannot Wait

When a family member, attorney, or business partner needs a signature from someone in custody, there is usually a deadline involved — real estate closings, court filings, power of attorney authorizations, or urgent family matters that cannot wait until release.

  • Powers of attorney so a spouse, family member, or attorney can act on the signer's behalf
  • Real estate documents including deeds, refinancing paperwork, and property transfers
  • Court filings, affidavits, and declarations for pending legal matters
  • Divorce and child custody documents that require both parties' signatures
  • Vehicle title transfers and DMV paperwork
  • Financial and business authorizations, including bank and investment account forms

What makes jail notarizations challenging

  • Strict facility schedules — visiting hours, lockdowns, and security procedures vary by jail and can change with little notice
  • Photo ID requirements — inmates may or may not have valid ID on file, and some facilities have their own identification verification procedures
  • Coordination with attorneys, family members, escrow officers, and courts who all need the signed documents by specific deadlines
  • Limited communication — the person in custody often cannot easily call, email, or review documents ahead of time

We handle the logistics so you can focus on getting the documents prepared and where they need to go.

How it works

Scheduling a Jail Notary Appointment

Before the appointment

  1. Contact us with the details — Call or text with the inmate's full legal name, booking number (if known), the facility where they are held, and a description of the documents that need to be notarized. Let us know your deadline.
  2. We coordinate with the facility — Each facility has different procedures for professional visits. We confirm visiting hours, security requirements, and any advance scheduling needed.
  3. Document preparation — Make sure the documents are fully prepared and ready to sign before the appointment. We can review documents ahead of time to confirm correct notary wording and signature blocks.

The appointment and after

  1. The notary appointment — We arrive at the facility, clear security, and meet with the inmate. The signer confirms their identity, signs the documents, and we complete the notarization with proper acknowledgment or jurat certificates.
  2. Document return — After the appointment, we return signed documents directly to you, your attorney, an escrow company, or another designated party — by hand delivery, courier, or mail depending on your timeline.

Frequently Asked Questions

Jail Notary FAQs

Can an inmate sign legal documents?

Yes. Being in custody does not remove a person's legal right to sign documents. As long as the signer is mentally competent, willing to sign, and can provide satisfactory identification, a notary can notarize their signature just like any other signing.

What ID does the inmate need?

Acceptable ID typically includes a valid driver's license, state ID, passport, or other government-issued photo ID. Some facilities can provide inmate identification verification through their booking records. We confirm ID options with you before the appointment.

Can I be present at the appointment?

This depends on the facility's rules. In most cases, professional notary visits are conducted separately from regular visitation. If you need to be present (for example, if both spouses must sign), we can check whether the facility allows this.

How far in advance should I schedule?

Ideally, give us at least 24–48 hours notice so we can coordinate with the facility. However, we understand jail notarizations are often urgent. Call or text as soon as you know you need the appointment, and we will do our best to accommodate your timeline.

What if the inmate is transferred or released before the appointment?

If we arrive at the facility and the inmate is no longer there, you may still be responsible for a trip fee. To reduce that risk, we recommend confirming the inmate's location close to the appointment time, especially if transfer or release is possible.

Do you notarize documents for federal detention facilities?

Federal facilities have different procedures and may require additional clearances. Contact us with the specific facility name and we can advise whether we can assist.
