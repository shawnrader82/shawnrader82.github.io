What we do

What Is Document Recording?

Recording is the act of filing a document with the county clerk-recorder so it becomes part of the permanent public record. For real property transfers — like a grant deed or quitclaim deed — recording legally establishes the new ownership. For business name filings, it satisfies California's publication requirement. Without recording, a deed can be valid between the parties but is invisible to lenders, title companies, and future buyers.

We pick up your document (or you can drop it off), verify it meets the recorder's formatting requirements, pay the filing fees on your behalf, and return the conformed copy with the recorder's official stamp — proving it's now on the public record.

How it works

Document Recording in 4 Steps.

From signed document to public record — we handle every step between notarization and the recorder's stamp.

1

Send Us Your Documents

Drop off your notarized document, have us come to you, or share a pre-signed package. We confirm it meets the recorder's formatting requirements before we go.

2

We Go to the Recorder

We visit the appropriate county branch, pay the filing fees on your behalf, and submit your document at the recording counter.

3

Document Is Stamped

The recorder assigns a document number, date-stamps the document, and adds it to the permanent public record. For deeds, this is when the title legally changes.

4

Conformed Copy Returned to You

We return the recorder-stamped conformed copy to you — your proof that the document is now on the public record. Keep this with your permanent files.

Important Limitations

  • No legal advice. We do not advise on which document type to use, whether a transfer triggers property tax reassessment, or the legal effect of any recording. Those questions belong to your attorney.
  • No foreign-recorded documents. We record California documents with California county recorders only. Documents meant for other states or foreign countries are outside our scope.
  • No quiet title or probate filings. Court-initiated actions like quiet title, probate petitions, or court-ordered recordings require an attorney and go through the court, not the recorder's window.
  • No document preparation. We record documents you provide. We do not draft, prepare, or review deeds or other legal instruments.
  • No guarantee of acceptance. If the recorder rejects a document for formatting or content reasons, we will notify you immediately. Rejection fees from the county are non-refundable but we will help you understand what needs to be corrected.

Common questions

Document Recording FAQ.

What is document recording?

Document recording is the process of officially filing a document with the county clerk-recorder so it becomes part of the permanent public record. For real property documents like deeds, recording establishes the legal change of ownership. For business documents like FBN statements, recording publishes your business name legally.

How much does document recording cost?

Our trip fee is $150 in LA County, $175 in Ventura County, and $200 in Orange County. County recording fees are pass-through costs paid directly to the recorder — use our live calculator on this page for an estimate. Each additional document is $25.

Do you notarize the document first or just record it?

Both services are available. If your document still needs notarization, we notarize it first and then take it to the recorder's office in the same trip. If it is already notarized and ready to file, we handle recording only.

Which county recorder offices do you serve?

LA County (Norwalk main office, Beverly Hills, Van Nuys, LAX corridor, and Lancaster branches), Orange County (Santa Ana main office and 3 branches), and Ventura County (Ventura recorder). We serve both the Clerk and Recorder functions at each office.

How long does recording take?

Most documents recorded at a counter walk-in appointment are stamped and returned same-day. We will return the conformed copy to you as soon as the recorder processes it, typically within 1–3 business days for mail-in documents.

Can you record documents you did not notarize?

Yes. If your document has already been notarized and is ready to file, we can pick it up and take it to the recorder. The document must be complete, properly acknowledged, and meet the county's formatting requirements.

What is the SB2 fee?

SB2 is California's Building Homes and Jobs Act fee — $75 per title recorded, capped at $225 per transaction. It applies to most real property documents except primary-residence purchase transactions and a few other exemptions.

Do you provide legal advice about which deed to use?

No. We are not attorneys and cannot advise on which document type to use or the legal consequences of recording. Those questions go to your real estate attorney or estate planning attorney. Once you have a document ready to record, we handle the filing.

What do you do after recording?

After the recorder accepts and stamps the document, we return the conformed (recorder-stamped) copy to you for your records. For some document types, the recorder mails the original back to the grantee — we will confirm which applies to your document type.

Can you get a certified copy of a document already on record?

Yes. If you need a certified copy of a deed or other document already recorded with the county, we can request it at the recorder's office for you. See our Certified Copies page for details.
