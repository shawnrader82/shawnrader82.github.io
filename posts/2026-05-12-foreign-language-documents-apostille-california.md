## Why Translation Matters Before an Apostille

Many overseas schools, consulates, courts, and government agencies will not accept English-only documents. Common requests include translated and apostilled copies of birth certificates, marriage certificates, school records, background checks, and powers of attorney.

If the translation is not prepared correctly, or if the translator’s statement is missing or not notarized when required, the receiving agency can reject the entire packet and send you back to the beginning.

## Step 1: Get a Proper Certified Translation

A certified translation is more than just someone who speaks both languages typing up the document. It includes a professional translation plus a signed statement from the translator confirming that the translation is complete and accurate.

That signed statement matters because many agencies, including consulates, schools, courts, and immigration-related offices, rely on it when deciding whether to accept the translation.

Here’s how the process usually starts:

- Send clear scans or PDFs of your documents.
- Confirm the language the documents are currently in and the language you need.
- Share where the documents will be used, such as USCIS, a consulate, a foreign university, or a court overseas.
- Let us know if you have a deadline, appointment date, or travel timeline.

Once the documents are reviewed, you can receive an estimate based on length, language pair, and complexity, along with an approximate turnaround time. Rush options may be available for some projects.

## Common Mistakes That Cause Delays

A few problems come up again and again in international document cases:

- Starting translation before checking the school, consulate, or agency instructions.
- Using a friend or relative instead of a certified translator.
- Skipping notarization of the translator’s declaration when it is required.
- Sending documents for apostille without confirming whether they must be notarized, certified, or reissued first.
- Waiting too long before a visa appointment, school deadline, or travel date.

The best way to avoid delays is to confirm the destination country, the receiving agency, and the exact type of document before anything is submitted.
