Frequently Asked Questions

The answers below describe our notary service and cite publicly available statutory or agency information. They are not legal advice. For questions about which form to use, what to write in it, or how the statute applies to your situation, please consult a licensed California attorney.

Do you notarize a Notice of Revocation of Power of Attorney?
Yes. Once your Notice of Revocation is completed and you are ready to sign, we can notarize your acknowledgment as a California Notary Public across Los Angeles, Orange, and Ventura Counties.
Can you help me decide whether revocation is the right step?
No. Whether to revoke a power of attorney, and how a particular revocation will affect existing relationships or transactions, are legal questions. Please consult a licensed California attorney. We can notarize a written revocation once you have prepared it.
Can you help me fill out the revocation form?
No. Under California law, a notary may not draft, complete, or advise on the content of a document. If you have questions about how to identify the original power of attorney, whom to name, or what to write in the blanks, please consult a licensed California attorney.
If the original power of attorney was recorded at a county recorder, can you record the revocation?
Yes. Once the revocation is notarized, we can take it to the appropriate county recorder through our Document Recording service. Whether recording is required or advisable in your particular situation is a legal and title-practice question we cannot answer — please direct that to your attorney or the title company involved.
Can you notarize a revocation at a hospital, care facility, or private home?
Yes. Mobile bedside notarization is a routine part of our service across Los Angeles, Orange, and Ventura Counties. The signer must be able to communicate directly with the notary, present valid ID, and sign without assistance.
Does revoking the power of attorney automatically notify my former agent and third parties?
No. Under California Probate Code section 4151 and the general law of agency, a revocation becomes effective as to third parties only when they receive actual notice of it. How to give effective notice in your specific situation is a legal question — please consult a licensed California attorney.
