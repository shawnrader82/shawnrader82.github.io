When You Would Use This Form

Every California adult should have an Advance Health Care Directive on file — not just seniors or terminally ill patients. Common situations where we help clients complete this:

  • Hospital admission planning: Before a scheduled surgery, cancer treatment, or extended hospitalization.
  • Aging parents: Setting up authority for adult children to make medical decisions when a parent is no longer able.
  • Terminal illness or hospice: Formalizing end-of-life wishes including DNR, feeding tube, and pain management preferences.
  • Estate planning: Part of a complete estate package alongside a Living Trust, Durable POA, and HIPAA authorization.
  • Pre-travel or pre-deployment: Setting up healthcare authority before an extended trip or military deployment.
  • New parents: Younger adults planning ahead in case of accident or medical emergency.

Under California law, if you have not signed an Advance Directive and you become incapacitated, medical decisions default to a hierarchy: spouse, then adult children (by majority), then parents, then siblings. If family disagrees, the hospital may seek a court-appointed conservator, which is expensive and slow. Signing the directive avoids that.

Who Can Notarize It

California Probate Code §4673 requires the Advance Health Care Directive to be either notarized OR signed in the presence of two qualified adult witnesses — not both required. Notarization is often easier because:

  • Two independent witnesses can be difficult to arrange, especially at a hospital bedside where family members are often disqualified.
  • Witnesses must not be the healthcare agent, the patient's healthcare provider, or an employee of the healthcare provider — which excludes most people in a hospital room.
  • A Notary can act as the sole authenticator, avoiding the witness eligibility problem entirely.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Naming co-agents who must agree. Deadlocks between co-agents can delay urgent medical decisions. Name one primary agent and successor agents instead.
  • Not giving copies to your care team. The directive is useless if your doctor and hospital do not have it on file. Give copies proactively before an emergency.
  • Storing the original in a safe deposit box. If you become incapacitated during banking hours, family may not have access. Keep the original at home in a known, accessible location.
  • Naming an agent who lives far away. Your agent should be reachable quickly in a medical emergency. Local family or friends are usually better choices than out-of-state relatives.
  • Not updating after major life events. Divorce, agent's death, or family conflict may require updating the directive. Review it every 5 years or after any major life change.
  • Trying to notarize with disqualified witnesses. If you use the two-witness route, neither witness can be the agent, the healthcare provider, or an employee of the healthcare facility. Notarization avoids this problem entirely.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the Advance Directive need to be notarized in California?
California Probate Code §4673 requires the directive to be either notarized OR signed by two qualified adult witnesses — not both. Notarization is often easier, especially at a hospital bedside, since two independent witnesses can be difficult to arrange (the witnesses cannot be your healthcare agent or healthcare provider).
Can I sign an Advance Directive at a hospital?
Yes. We provide hospital bedside notary service across Los Angeles, Orange, and Ventura Counties. This is one of the most common situations where we notarize Advance Directives — when a patient is being admitted, before surgery, or during end-of-life care planning.
What is the difference between an Advance Directive and a Living Will?
In California, the Advance Health Care Directive is a combined document that includes both the health care power of attorney (naming an agent) and the living will (specifying end-of-life wishes). It replaced the separate documents that existed before 2000. Some other states still use separate documents.
Can I name multiple healthcare agents?
Yes, but only one can act at a time. The California form lets you name a primary agent and up to two successor agents (in order of priority). If the primary agent is unavailable, the first successor takes over. You should NOT name co-agents who must agree, because deadlocks in medical emergencies are dangerous.
Can my healthcare agent be a family member?
Yes. Most people name a spouse, adult child, or sibling. The main restriction is that the agent cannot be your treating healthcare provider or an employee of the facility where you are being treated (unless they are also a relative).
Should I file this with my doctor or hospital?
Yes. Give copies to your primary care doctor, any specialists, your named agent, your successor agents, and any hospital where you are likely to receive care. Keep the original in a safe but accessible place — not in a safe deposit box, since it needs to be reachable in an emergency.
