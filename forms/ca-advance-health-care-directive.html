Frequently Asked Questions

The answers below describe our notary service and cite publicly available statutory or agency information. They are not legal advice. For questions about which form to use, what to write in it, or how the statute applies to your situation, please consult a licensed California attorney.

Do you notarize the California Advance Health Care Directive?
Yes. Once the form is completed and you are ready to sign, we can notarize your acknowledgment as a California Notary Public across Los Angeles, Orange, and Ventura Counties, including at a hospital bedside.
Can you notarize an Advance Directive at a hospital or care facility?
Yes. Hospital bedside notarization is a routine part of our service. The signer must be able to communicate directly with the notary, present valid ID, and sign without assistance. See our hospital notary page for how bedside signings work.
Can you also serve as one of the two witnesses instead of notarizing?
California Probate Code §4673 sets specific qualifications for who may serve as a witness. Whether a particular person qualifies is a legal question. If you are considering the two-witness pathway rather than notarization, please consult a licensed California attorney about witness qualifications. Where notarization is used, no witnesses are required by the statute.
Can you help me choose an agent or fill out the form?
No. Under California law, a notary may not draft, complete, or advise on the content of a document. Choosing an agent, selecting end-of-life preferences, and completing the form are legal questions. Please consult a licensed California attorney if you have questions about the content.
Can multiple family members be notarized in one visit?
Yes. We frequently notarize signatures for spouses or family members together in a single visit. Each signer must present valid, unexpired, government-issued photo ID and must sign in front of the notary.
Can I revoke or amend a signed Advance Directive later?
The Advance Health Care Directive can be revoked or amended under California Probate Code Division 4.7. How to revoke effectively and what notice to provide is a legal question — please consult a licensed California attorney. Once you have prepared a written revocation or amendment, we can notarize it.
