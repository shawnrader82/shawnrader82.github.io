When You Would Use This Form

You would typically need a Philippine SPA when you are physically in the U.S. and need to authorize a representative (usually a family member or trusted person) in the Philippines to handle a specific matter for you. The most common situations we see in Los Angeles:

  • Real estate: Selling, buying, mortgaging, or transferring title to Philippine property.
  • Bank matters: Closing an account, transferring funds, or handling a deceased relative's bank accounts.
  • Land title transfers: Inheritance, extrajudicial settlement, or property partition.
  • Court appearances: When you cannot travel to the Philippines for a hearing.
  • Business filings: Signing corporate documents, tax filings, or SEC/DTI paperwork.
  • Passport & civil registry: Handling PSA birth certificate corrections, marriage record updates, or NBI clearance retrieval.

The Philippines joined the Hague Apostille Convention on May 14, 2019, which means an SPA notarized in California and Apostilled by the California Secretary of State is now accepted directly by Philippine authorities — no more red-ribbon consular authentication required. Who Can Notarize It

Two options for Filipinos in Los Angeles:

  • California Notary Public (this is us). We notarize the SPA in Los Angeles, then Apostille it through the California Secretary of State. This is typically faster, more flexible, and avoids the wait for a consulate appointment. Total turnaround: often 3–5 business days including same-day Apostille service.
  • Philippine Consulate General Los Angeles. The consulate can notarize (called consular acknowledgment) directly, which does not require Apostille. However, appointments can be weeks out, and you must appear in person at their office in downtown LA. Their forms page lists what they accept.

Which is better? California notarization + Apostille is usually faster and can be done at your home, office, hospital bedside, or anywhere convenient. Consular notarization avoids the Apostille step but requires you to travel to the consulate.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Blanks left in the form. The SPA has numbered lines (1-6) where you must list the specific acts you are authorizing. "Handle my property" is not specific enough — write out each act (e.g., "Sign the deed of absolute sale for the property located at [address]").
  • Signing before the notary. Do not sign the SPA before we arrive. California law requires the signer to sign in the notary's presence.
  • Wrong witness format. The consulate template does not require witnesses, but Philippine banks and land registries sometimes require two. Ask your Philippine agent what their receiving institution requires.
  • Missing Apostille. A notarized SPA alone is not enough — Philippine authorities now require the California Secretary of State Apostille attached before the document is legally recognized in the Philippines.
  • Expired ID. Bring a valid, unexpired, government-issued photo ID (driver's license, passport, or state ID). Expired IDs cannot be used for notarization in California.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to sign my SPA in front of the Philippine Consulate, or can a California Notary do it?
Since the Philippines joined the Hague Apostille Convention in May 2019, a California Notary can notarize your SPA and the California Secretary of State can Apostille it. The Apostilled SPA is then accepted directly in the Philippines with no further consular authentication needed. This is usually faster and more convenient than a consulate appointment.
How long does the full SPA + Apostille process take?
We can notarize the SPA the same day you contact us in most cases. The California Apostille through the LA Regional Office is often available same-day; the Sacramento office typically takes 3-5 business days by mail. Total turnaround: as fast as 1-2 business days for urgent matters, 3-7 business days for standard service.
Does the SPA need to be in English, Tagalog, or both?
The Philippine Consulate template is in English, which is accepted by Philippine courts, banks, and land registries. Tagalog translation is not required for the Apostille to be valid. If your specific receiving institution in the Philippines requires Tagalog, we can coordinate a certified translation.
Can my Philippine SPA authorize multiple acts, or do I need one SPA per matter?
A single SPA can authorize multiple specific acts to the same agent. Simply list each act clearly (1, 2, 3, etc.) on the form. However, if you are authorizing different agents for different matters (e.g., one relative for real estate, another for bank accounts), you will need separate SPAs.
What if my agent in the Philippines needs to substitute another person mid-transaction?
The Philippine Consulate SPA template includes standard "power of substitution" language, which allows your agent to delegate authority to another person if needed. Verify this clause is included and not struck through before signing.
Do you notarize Philippine SPAs at hospitals or nursing homes in Los Angeles?
Yes. We are a mobile Notary and routinely handle hospital bedside signings across LA, Orange, and Ventura Counties. This is especially common when an elderly Filipino parent needs to authorize an adult child in the Philippines to handle affairs. See our hospital notary page for details.
