When You Would Use This Form

You would use a California Statutory Power of Attorney when you want to authorize someone (usually a family member, spouse, or trusted friend) to handle specific financial matters on your behalf. Common situations:

  • Real estate transactions: When you cannot be present for a closing or property signing.
  • Banking and investments: Managing accounts, moving funds, or handling investment decisions during travel or illness.
  • Tax preparation and filing: Authorizing an accountant or family member to file on your behalf.
  • Business operations: Signing contracts, checks, or corporate documents during your absence.
  • Elder planning: Setting up financial authority for an adult child to help manage a parent's affairs.
  • Pre-deployment planning: Military service members setting up financial authority before deployment.

The statutory form lets you initial specific categories of authority (real property, tangible personal property, stocks and bonds, banks, business operations, insurance, estates, claims and litigation, personal and family maintenance, benefits from Social Security, retirement plans, and tax matters) rather than granting blanket authority.

Who Can Notarize It

California Probate Code §4402 requires the statutory POA to be either notarized OR signed by two adult witnesses (not both required — though many attorneys recommend both to strengthen enforceability). For real estate matters, notarization is required to record the POA with the county recorder.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Failing to initial category boxes. The statutory form only authorizes categories you specifically initial. Blank categories are not granted, which can create problems if your agent tries to act on your behalf later.
  • Naming an agent who is unavailable. The named agent must be reachable and willing to act. Always confirm with your agent before naming them, and consider naming a successor agent in case the first is unable.
  • Signing before the notary arrives. California law requires the signer to sign in the notary's presence. If you have already signed, we cannot notarize it and you will need a fresh copy.
  • Using an outdated form version. The California statutory form has been updated multiple times. Always use the current version from the official Probate Code source.
  • Not planning for real estate recording. If the POA will be used for real estate, record it at the county recorder where the property is located before the transaction. Otherwise the title company may reject it.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a California Statutory POA need to be notarized?
California Probate Code §4402 requires the POA to be either notarized OR signed by two adult witnesses. However, if the POA authorizes real estate transactions or will be recorded at a county recorder's office, notarization is required. Most attorneys recommend notarization regardless of use case for stronger enforceability.
How is a Statutory POA different from a Durable POA?
The Statutory POA is a specific pre-formatted form under California Probate Code §4401. It becomes durable (surviving your incapacity) unless you specifically opt out on the form. So a properly completed Statutory POA is by default a Durable POA. "Durable" simply means it stays in effect if you become mentally incapacitated.
Can I limit which financial matters my agent can handle?
Yes. The statutory form has 12 specific categories, and you initial only the ones you want to authorize. You can grant authority for banking but not real estate, for example. You can also add specific limitations or instructions in the "Special Instructions" section.
When does the POA go into effect?
By default, the POA is effective immediately upon signing. You can also make it a "springing" POA that only takes effect if a doctor certifies you are incapacitated — but this often causes delays because banks require the doctor's letter before honoring the POA.
How can I revoke a Power of Attorney?
Sign a written revocation and deliver it to your agent and any institution that has a copy of the POA (banks, brokerages, etc.). If the original POA was recorded at a county recorder's office, the revocation should also be recorded. We can notarize the revocation and coordinate recording if needed.
Do I need a lawyer to prepare this form?
Not required, but recommended if your situation is complex — for example, if you have significant assets, out-of-state property, business interests, or family disputes. For simple financial delegation between spouses or immediate family, the statutory form is designed to be self-completable. We can notarize either version.
