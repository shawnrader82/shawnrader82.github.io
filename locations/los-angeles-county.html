About this county

What "Los Angeles County" means for your document

Los Angeles County is the most populous county in the United States, with 88 incorporated cities and many unincorporated communities spread across roughly 4,000 square miles. For a notarization or apostille, the county itself usually doesn't change what's required — California law applies throughout the state — but it does affect where we travel, which facility a signing happens in, and which county office issues certain underlying records.

California rules, county logistics

A California-commissioned notary follows the same statute and journal rules anywhere in the state. What changes county-to-county is travel time, parking, hospital access policies, and jail visiting schedules — not the notarial act itself.

County-issued records often need apostille

LA County birth, death, and marriage certificates issued by the Registrar-Recorder/County Clerk are common apostille targets. We can explain the certified-copy path and the California Secretary of State step before you order anything.

Hospital & jail signings are coordinated

Hospitals and Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department facilities have their own visiting and documentation rules. We coordinate by phone first so the appointment time, ID requirements, and facility access expectations are clear before we travel.

County records & apostille

Los Angeles County vital records and apostille

Many apostille requests start with a Los Angeles County document — a birth certificate, a death certificate, or a marriage record. Here's how the pieces fit together so you don't order the wrong copy.

Birth, death, marriage

LA County issues certified copies through the Registrar-Recorder/County Clerk in Norwalk, with select branch offices. For apostille purposes, you generally need a certified copy with a wet-ink signature from the county clerk, not a copy you notarize yourself.

More: vital records apostille overview.

Divorce decrees

Divorce judgments come from the LA County Superior Court, not the Registrar-Recorder. You'll typically need a certified court copy with a wet-ink clerk's signature before the California Secretary of State can apostille it.

More: divorce decree apostille.

State-level vital records

For births, deaths, or marriages outside LA County, the California Department of Public Health (CDPH) issues state-level certified copies. Those work for apostille too, but typically have longer processing times than county-issued copies.

What we handle

Once you have the correct certified copy, we coordinate the California Secretary of State apostille step. For documents that aren't government-issued (powers of attorney, affidavits, corporate records, school records), we handle the notarization first and then the apostille.
